# Philosophical Inquiry

## Goal
Investigate a philosophical question by examining assumptions, constructing and dismantling arguments, and arriving at a position through the process of reasoning itself.

## Quality Signals
- The question is stated sharply -- no fuzzy "what is the nature of X?" without specificity
- Opposing positions are steelmanned before being critiqued
- Thought experiments or concrete scenarios ground abstract claims
- The writing itself enacts philosophical thinking: questioning, pausing, reconsidering
- A position is taken and defended, even if provisional

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT survey philosophers like a textbook ("Kant said X, Hegel said Y, Nietzsche said Z")
- DO NOT end with "it's a matter of perspective" -- commit to an argument
- DO NOT use heavy formatting (bullets, bold, tables) -- philosophy lives in paragraphs
- DO NOT separate the philosophical content from the writing style -- the prose IS the thought

## Gold Standard
"What does it mean to deserve something? We say the hardworking student deserves her grades. But what made her hardworking? Discipline, perhaps. And where did the discipline come from? Upbringing, genetics, circumstance -- none of which she chose. Pull the thread far enough and desert dissolves into luck. Rawls saw this clearly: behind the veil of ignorance, nobody would design a system that rewards accidents of birth. And yet. Strip away desert entirely and you face a different problem: a world where nothing you do matters, because your actions were never really yours. The tension isn't between justice and injustice. It's between two kinds of injustice."
