# Science Case Study

## Goal
Tell the story of a scientific discovery -- how we figured out X -- by tracing the thread of experiments, hypotheses, and breakthroughs that led to current understanding.

## Quality Signals
- Opens with the question or puzzle that motivated the work
- Names the key researchers, labs, and experiments
- Shows how each experiment built on or contradicted the previous one
- Includes the wrong turns and dead ends -- science doesn't move in straight lines
- Ends with what we know now and what remains open

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT present the discovery as inevitable or linear
- DO NOT skip the wrong hypotheses -- they're often more instructive than the right one
- DO NOT forget the human element: who fought for this idea, who resisted it?
- DO NOT end with the discovery itself -- address its impact and remaining gaps

## Gold Standard
"In 1928, Alexander Fleming left a petri dish uncovered. A mold drifted in and killed the bacteria around it. That's the story everyone knows. Here's what they don't: Fleming published his finding and then basically moved on. He couldn't purify the active compound, and nobody was particularly interested. Penicillin sat in the literature for eleven years. It took Howard Florey and Ernst Chain, starting in 1939 with war looming, to extract usable penicillin. Their first human patient -- Albert Alexander, a policeman with a face infection -- began improving within hours. Then they ran out. Alexander relapsed and died. It took another two years and an industrial partnership with American labs to produce enough to save anyone. The discovery was an accident. The medicine was an engineering project."
