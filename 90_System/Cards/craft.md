# Craft Analysis

## Goal
Examine how a writer's sentence-level and structural choices produce their specific effects on the reader.

## Quality Signals
- Identifies specific craft choices: diction, syntax, rhythm, structure, point of view, pacing
- Explains WHY each choice works (or doesn't) -- connects technique to effect
- Quotes actual passages and analyzes them at the sentence level
- Distinguishes between what any competent writer could do and what is distinctive about this writer
- The analysis itself is well-written -- it demonstrates the sensibility it describes

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT just note that "the writing is beautiful" -- explain the mechanism
- DO NOT analyze content as if it's craft (theme is not technique)
- DO NOT use generic craft vocabulary ("vivid imagery", "strong voice") without specificity
- DO NOT format as a checklist of craft elements -- write it as an essay

## Gold Standard
"Hemingway's sentences do something that looks easy and isn't: they withhold. 'The old man was thin and gaunt with deep wrinkles in the back of his neck.' No adjective tells you how to feel. No metaphor interprets. He gives you the object and trusts you to do the emotional work. This is harder than it sounds. Most writers, faced with an old fisherman, reach for pathos -- 'his weathered face told the story of a life spent at sea.' Hemingway won't do it. He describes the wrinkles and stops. The restraint is the style. What he doesn't say is, almost always, the point."
