# Social Case Analysis

## Goal
Analyze a real-world system, policy, organization, or decision by identifying who benefits, who pays, and what happens next.

## Quality Signals
- The system/policy is described in terms of its stated goals AND its actual effects
- The gap between intention and outcome is analyzed structurally, not morally
- Key stakeholders are identified with their incentives mapped
- Data and evidence are used to support claims
- Counter-arguments and alternative explanations are addressed

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT accept the system's stated goals as its actual function -- analyze the gap
- DO NOT analyze in isolation -- compare to alternative systems or historical precedents
- DO NOT moralize without mechanism -- if something is bad, explain WHY structurally it fails
- DO NOT stop at description -- provide analytical framework for evaluating the system

## Gold Standard
"Uber's surge pricing is, on paper, an elegant supply-demand mechanism. When demand exceeds supply, prices rise, which simultaneously discourages marginal riders and incentivizes more drivers to get on the road. Equilibrium is restored. Clean. Logical. Now watch what actually happens. During a hostage crisis in Sydney in 2014, surge pricing kicked in automatically. People fleeing the area saw fares quadruple. Uber's algorithm couldn't distinguish between 'many people want rides to the concert' and 'many people are fleeing for their lives.' The mechanism worked perfectly. The outcome was monstrous. This is the fundamental limitation of price-based allocation: it optimizes for efficiency, and efficiency is amoral."
