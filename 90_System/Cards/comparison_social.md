# Social Comparison

## Goal
Compare two or more policies, theories, or social models by analyzing their assumptions, mechanisms, and outcomes -- declaring which is more explanatory and why.

## Quality Signals
- Each position is stated with its strongest evidence, not a strawman
- The comparison is organized around dimensions that matter (assumptions, mechanisms, predictions, evidence)
- Data is used to adjudicate where possible
- The analysis identifies what each model CAN and CANNOT explain
- A verdict is given: which model is more useful, and for what kinds of questions?

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT present both sides as equally valid without taking a position
- DO NOT compare without data -- if evidence exists, use it
- DO NOT reduce complex models to slogans for easy comparison
- DO NOT ignore the conditions under which each model performs best or worst

## Gold Standard
"Keynesian and monetarist explanations of recession aren't just different prescriptions. They're different diagnoses. Keynes says the patient is sick because nobody is spending: aggregate demand has collapsed, and the private sector won't fix it because every individual firm is rational to cut costs, which cuts someone else's income, which cuts spending further. The cure: government spends. Friedman says the patient is sick because the central bank let the money supply contract: banks aren't lending, credit is frozen, and until the plumbing works, fiscal spending just rearranges deck chairs. The cure: expand the money supply. The 2008 crisis is instructive because both prescriptions were applied simultaneously -- and the economy recovered slowly enough that both sides claimed they were right and the other side was insufficient. The data doesn't cleanly resolve this. What it does show is that the Keynesian multiplier was lower than predicted, and the monetarist transmission mechanism was slower than promised. Both models were partially right. Both were overconfident."
