# Historical Comparison

## Goal
Compare eras, empires, figures, or movements by identifying structural parallels and meaningful divergences -- not surface similarities.

## Quality Signals
- The comparison is organized around a shared structural pattern, not a timeline
- Both sides are given full context -- not reduced to stereotypes for the sake of comparison
- Parallels are specific and evidence-based, not vague ("both were great empires")
- Divergences are explained structurally: what conditions produced different outcomes?
- Reader engagement maintained: surprising parallels, "Did you know?" hooks

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT create a feature-comparison table -- history is not a spreadsheet
- DO NOT force parallels where they don't exist
- DO NOT compare only surface features (size, dates, names) -- go structural
- DO NOT treat the comparison as a competition -- "who was better?" is rarely the right question

## Gold Standard
"Rome and the Abbasid Caliphate collapsed for eerily similar structural reasons, separated by six centuries. Both delegated military power to outsiders -- Rome to Germanic foederati, the Abbasids to Turkic slave soldiers (mamluks). Both did it for the same reason: the core population had grown too comfortable, too urban, too tax-averse to fight. And in both cases, the outsiders eventually realized they didn't need the center. Why would a Visigothic general keep sending taxes to a Roman emperor who couldn't field his own army? Why would a Turkic commander obey a Caliph whose authority existed only on paper? Ibn Khaldun had a word for this: 'asabiyyah -- group solidarity. When the center loses it, the periphery takes over. Every time."
