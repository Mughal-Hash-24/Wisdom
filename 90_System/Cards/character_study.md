# Character Study

## Goal
Analyze a literary character by examining their motivations, arc, symbolic function, and the authorial choices that construct them.

## Quality Signals
- Distinguishes between what the character says and what the author is doing with them
- Traces the character's arc through specific scenes and turning points
- Identifies the character's function within the narrative's larger argument
- Uses direct textual evidence: quotes, described actions, symbolic moments
- Addresses what the character reveals about the world the author has constructed

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT psychoanalyze the character as if they're a real person -- they're a construction
- DO NOT write a biography (born, grew up, did X, then Y) -- focus on meaning, not chronology
- DO NOT ignore the gap between the character's self-understanding and the reader's understanding
- DO NOT use heavy formatting -- character analysis is an essay, not a profile card

## Gold Standard
"Michael Corleone's transformation is almost too perfect, and that's precisely the point. Puzo constructs him as the family's escape hatch -- the war hero, the Ivy League son, the one who was supposed to go legitimate. Then he reverses every marker, one by one, in a sequence so methodical it reads like an argument. The restaurant killing doesn't just change Michael. It reveals that the capacity was always there, sleeping beneath the officer's uniform. When Michael tells Kay 'I'm not part of that,' in the wedding scene, Puzo is not showing us a man who is lying. He's showing us a man who hasn't yet discovered what he is."
