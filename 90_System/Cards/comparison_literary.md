# Literary Comparison

## Goal
Compare two or more authors, works, or literary traditions by examining how they solve the same creative problems differently.

## Quality Signals
- The comparison is organized around a shared problem or question, not a feature checklist
- Direct textual evidence from both works supports every claim
- Differences are explained in terms of worldview, tradition, and artistic intent -- not ranked as better/worse
- The comparison reveals something about each work that wouldn't be visible alone
- The prose itself is literary -- this is criticism, not a spreadsheet

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT create a comparison table -- literature is not a product spec
- DO NOT write two separate analyses and call it a comparison -- the works must be in dialogue
- DO NOT compare only surface features (setting, plot, character count) -- go deeper
- DO NOT avoid judgment -- if one work achieves something the other doesn't, say so

## Gold Standard
"Dostoevsky and Tolstoy both write about guilt, but they locate it in entirely different places. For Dostoevsky, guilt is internal and metaphysical -- Raskolnikov's crime tears something in his soul that no social consequence can repair. For Tolstoy, guilt is social and embodied -- Anna Karenina's suffering comes not from what she's done but from what society does to her for having done it. Dostoevsky's characters need God. Tolstoy's characters need forgiveness from the people around them. The same word -- guilt -- means two completely different things in their hands, and the novels they build around it are, as a result, almost opposite architectures wearing the same label."
