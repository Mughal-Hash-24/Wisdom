# Aesthetic Analysis

## Goal
Analyze a work of visual art, music, or architecture by connecting its technical choices to their emotional and intellectual effects.

## Quality Signals
- The work is described so vividly the reader can see or hear it through language alone
- Technical vocabulary of the medium is used precisely (not generically)
- Each technical choice is connected to an effect: WHY does this composition/harmony/structure work?
- The work is placed in its tradition: what came before, what it responds to
- The analysis respects the medium -- doesn't reduce visual art to narrative or music to emotion

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT inventory features ("the painting uses warm colors, strong lines, and contrast")
- DO NOT separate technique from meaning -- every paragraph should connect both
- DO NOT use vague aesthetic language ("beautiful", "powerful", "moving") without explaining the mechanism
- DO NOT format as a bulleted list of observations -- write as flowing prose

## Gold Standard
"The first chord of Beethoven's Eroica is not a chord. It's a declaration of war. Two E-flat major stabs, fortissimo, full orchestra -- and then, before the listener can settle, the cellos begin a theme that sounds like it should be simple but isn't. It outlines the tonic triad and then wanders to a C-sharp that has no business being there. In two bars, Beethoven has established a key and then undermined it. That C-sharp is a splinter. The next forty minutes are spent either working it out or pretending it isn't there. This is the structural logic of the Eroica: it doesn't resolve tension. It metabolizes it."
