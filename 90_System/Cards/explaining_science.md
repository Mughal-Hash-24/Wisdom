# Explaining Science

## Goal
Explain a scientific mechanism so the reader understands the chain of cause and effect at the molecular, cellular, or systemic level.

## Quality Signals
- Opens with the observable phenomenon or real-world question that makes the mechanism relevant
- Traces the causal chain step by step: what triggers what, and why
- Distinguishes established knowledge from current hypotheses -- marks the boundary explicitly
- References key experiments or discoveries that established the mechanism
- Makes microscopic processes tangible through analogy or vivid description

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT present a textbook definition and stop there -- trace the mechanism
- DO NOT say "scientists believe" without specifying what evidence supports the belief
- DO NOT use jargon without defining it on first use
- DO NOT skip the "how do we know this?" question -- name the experiment

## Gold Standard
"Your cells are committing suicide right now. About 50 to 70 billion of them will die today, deliberately, on schedule. It's called apoptosis, and it's not a malfunction -- it's maintenance. A cell receives a death signal (from neighboring cells, from internal damage sensors, or from the immune system), and instead of bursting messily like a necrotic cell, it dismantles itself from the inside. The mitochondria release cytochrome c, which activates caspases -- molecular scissors that cut the cell's proteins and DNA into neat fragments. The remains are wrapped in membrane and consumed by macrophages. No inflammation. No mess. The reason cancer is terrifying isn't that cells grow too fast. It's that they've forgotten how to die."
