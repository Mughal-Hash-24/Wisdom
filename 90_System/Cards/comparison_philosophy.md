# Philosophical Comparison

## Goal
Compare two or more philosophical schools, frameworks, or thinkers by engaging with the ideas themselves -- not by listing features side by side.

## Quality Signals
- Each position is stated in its strongest form before comparison
- The comparison is organized around genuine disagreements, not superficial differences
- The analysis identifies what each framework CAN and CANNOT explain
- A judgment is offered: which framework is more explanatory, and for what kinds of questions?
- The writing reads as philosophical argument, not encyclopedia

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT create a feature-comparison table -- philosophy is not a product spec
- DO NOT present both sides as equally valid without defending a position
- DO NOT reduce complex thinkers to slogans ("Kant = duty, Mill = outcomes")
- DO NOT use heavy markdown formatting -- this is a philosophical essay

## Gold Standard
"Utilitarianism gives you a calculator. Deontology gives you a compass. The calculator is powerful: it can weigh consequences, optimize outcomes, measure suffering in units you can almost quantify. But it also says, in principle, that torturing one innocent to save a thousand is not just permissible but obligatory. The compass refuses. Kant says the torture is wrong regardless of outcomes, because the person on the table is not a means to your arithmetic. The utilitarian finds this maddening. The deontologist finds the calculating monstrous. Neither is wrong. But notice what each framework is quietly assuming: the utilitarian assumes consequences are knowable. The deontologist assumes duties are universal. Both assumptions crack under pressure."
