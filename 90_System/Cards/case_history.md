# Historical Case Study

## Goal
Analyze how a specific event, decision, or crisis reshaped the political, social, or economic landscape that followed it.

## Quality Signals
- The event is placed in its BEFORE context: what was the status quo?
- The decision-makers, their constraints, and their information are specified
- The immediate consequences are traced
- Second and third-order effects are followed: what changed that nobody expected?
- The analysis connects the event to longer-term structural shifts
- Reader engagement: "Here's what most people miss:", lesser-known ripple effects

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT just describe the event -- analyze its structural impact
- DO NOT present the event as inevitable -- show the contingencies
- DO NOT stop at immediate consequences -- trace the chain further
- DO NOT ignore the political, economic, and social dimensions in favor of just one

## Gold Standard
"The Berlin Conference of 1884 lasted three months. No African attended. Fourteen European powers drew borders across a continent they largely hadn't explored, splitting ethnic groups, combining rivals, and creating administrative units that made sense only from the perspective of a conference table in Bismarck's chancellery. Here's what most people miss: the conference didn't just divide Africa. It created the template for how Europe would govern it. The borders required centralized states to enforce them. The centralized states required bureaucracies. The bureaucracies required educated locals. And the educated locals, within two generations, would use the very institutions colonialism built to demand its end. The conference planted the seeds of its own undoing. It just took seventy years for them to grow."
