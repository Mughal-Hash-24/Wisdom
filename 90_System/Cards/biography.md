# Biography

## Goal
Analyze a historical figure by examining the key moments, motivations, and structural conditions that made them who they were -- not as a timeline, but as an argument about a person.

## Quality Signals
- Opens with a defining moment or revealing detail, not birth date
- Distinguishes between what the person said about themselves and what the evidence shows
- Identifies the structural conditions (era, class, geography) that shaped their choices
- Key turning points are analyzed, not just listed
- Lesser-known facts and personal details humanize the figure
- Engages the reader: "Why do you think...?", "Here's what most biographies leave out:"

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT write a chronological biography (born, grew up, achieved X, died)
- DO NOT hagiographize -- show flaws, contradictions, and failures
- DO NOT ignore the context that made the person's rise possible
- DO NOT present the person's legacy as settled -- show how different eras have interpreted them differently

## Gold Standard
"Here's what most people get wrong about Jinnah: they think he always wanted Pakistan. He didn't. For twenty years, Muhammad Ali Jinnah was the greatest advocate for Hindu-Muslim unity in Indian politics. Sarojini Naidu called him 'the ambassador of Hindu-Muslim unity' -- and she meant it. So what happened? Congress happened. Or more precisely, Congress's refusal to share power happened. After the 1937 provincial elections, Congress swept into power and governed as if the Muslim League didn't exist. Jinnah watched his lifelong project -- joint governance -- get dismantled by the people he'd spent decades working with. Did he want partition? Or did he conclude it was the only card left to play? The answer matters, because it changes the entire story."
