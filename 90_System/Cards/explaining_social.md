# Explaining Social Mechanisms

## Goal
Explain how a social, economic, or psychological mechanism works by tracing the incentive structure, the behavior it produces, and the second-order effects.

## Quality Signals
- Opens with who the actors are and what they want
- The incentive structure is stated explicitly: what rewards and punishes what behavior?
- Second-order effects are traced: "And then what?"
- Data, numbers, or studies are cited where available
- The explanation resists surface-level narratives: "it's more complicated than it looks"

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT explain behavior without identifying the incentive driving it
- DO NOT stop at first-order effects -- the interesting part is always the next link in the chain
- DO NOT use vague qualitative claims ("this increases inequality") without data or mechanism
- DO NOT moralize -- describe the system, then evaluate it

## Gold Standard
"Insurance creates a paradox: the act of protecting someone from risk changes their relationship to risk. An uninsured driver checks their mirrors. An insured driver checks their phone. This is moral hazard, and it's not a flaw in insurance design -- it's a fundamental property of any system that separates consequences from actions. The insurance company knows this, which is why they invented the deductible. A deductible is a calibrated re-introduction of risk: enough to make you careful, not enough to bankrupt you. The entire structure of insurance pricing is a negotiation between two competing goals: protection and accountability. Every dollar of deductible shifts the balance."
