# Critical Reading

## Goal
Analyze a literary work by examining its choices, strengths, blind spots, and implications -- producing insight the reader didn't have before.

## Quality Signals
- Quotes or cites specific moments FROM the work (scenes, lines, images, structural choices)
- Takes a clear interpretive position and defends it with textual evidence
- Acknowledges counter-readings before arguing against them
- Connects the specific work to broader literary traditions or cultural contexts
- The reader finishes with a sharper understanding than they started with

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT summarize the plot and call it analysis
- DO NOT apply frameworks that don't fit (game theory on poetry, timeline tables on character arcs)
- DO NOT hedge every claim with "perhaps" and "arguably" -- commit to an interpretation
- DO NOT write in textbook register -- this is criticism, not a book report

## Gold Standard
"Fitzgerald does something ruthless with the green light. He gives Gatsby -- and the reader -- a symbol so seductive that we invest in it completely. Then he reveals what it actually is: a dock light at the end of someone else's pier. The entire architecture of longing in the novel rests on this bait-and-switch. We want the green light to mean something transcendent because Gatsby wants it to. And when Nick tells us it was always just a light, we feel the loss personally. That's not symbolism. That's narrative entrapment. Fitzgerald doesn't describe the American Dream's failure. He makes you live it."
