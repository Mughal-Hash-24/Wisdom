# Thought Experiment

## Goal
Use a carefully constructed hypothetical scenario to reveal a deep truth about physics, philosophy, or logic that is difficult to see through direct argument alone.

## Quality Signals
- The scenario is precisely defined: all relevant conditions are specified
- The reasoning proceeds step by step from the setup to the surprising conclusion
- The conclusion challenges an intuition or reveals a hidden assumption
- Objections and counter-scenarios are addressed
- The connection to the broader theory or debate is made explicit

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT present a vague scenario with hand-wavy reasoning
- DO NOT skip the step where we identify WHICH intuition is being violated
- DO NOT leave the thought experiment hanging -- draw the conclusion explicitly
- DO NOT make the scenario more complex than it needs to be

## Gold Standard
"Imagine a sealed box with a cat, a radioactive atom, and a Geiger counter wired to a vial of poison. Quantum mechanics says the atom is in superposition: decayed and not-decayed simultaneously. If the atom's state is indefinite, and the cat's fate depends on it, is the cat alive and dead simultaneously? Schrodinger didn't propose this to celebrate quantum weirdness. He proposed it to expose what he saw as its absurdity. The question isn't 'is the cat both alive and dead?' The question is: at what point does quantum indefiniteness become classical definiteness? That boundary -- the measurement problem -- remains unsolved. The cat was never the point. The point was the question nobody could answer."
