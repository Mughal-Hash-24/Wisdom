# Game Theory

## Goal
Analyze a strategic interaction by identifying players, strategies, payoffs, and equilibria -- then connect the model to real-world behavior.

## Quality Signals
- Players, strategies, and payoffs are clearly defined
- The equilibrium (Nash, dominant strategy, mixed) is identified and explained intuitively
- The gap between theoretical prediction and real-world behavior is discussed
- The model's assumptions are stated and their limitations acknowledged
- Real-world examples illustrate the strategic dynamics

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT present the payoff matrix without explaining what it means intuitively
- DO NOT stop at the equilibrium -- discuss whether people actually play that way (and why not)
- DO NOT ignore the assumptions: repeated game vs one-shot, complete vs incomplete information
- DO NOT apply game theory where it doesn't fit -- not every interaction is a strategic game

## Gold Standard
"The Five Families operated a textbook oligopoly. Each family controlled territory -- and territory meant revenue. Expansion was possible but expensive: war costs soldiers, draws police attention, and disrupts the business everyone depends on. The equilibrium was therefore mutual restraint: you stay out of my territory, I stay out of yours, and we all make money. The Commission existed to enforce this equilibrium -- not through trust, but through the credible threat of collective retaliation against any family that broke the peace. This is the logic of mutually assured destruction, applied to organized crime. It held for decades. It collapsed when Sonny Corleone's temper broke the commitment mechanism -- a single undisciplined player can destroy a cooperative equilibrium that benefits everyone."
