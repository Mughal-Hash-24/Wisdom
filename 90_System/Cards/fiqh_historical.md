# Fiqh Historical Card

## Goal
Trace how this school's ruling on this question developed across generations — from the founding jurists to the classical period to later scholarship. The history of juristic reasoning is the deliverable.

## Output Requirements

1. **Ruling** — state the current dominant (mu'tamad) position briefly.
2. **Usul al-Fiqh** — the derivation of the *current* ruling, following the standard structure.
3. **Historical Development** — this section replaces or substantially expands the Notes section:
   - When did this school's jurists first encounter this question?
   - What was the founding generation's position (Abu Hanifa / Malik / Al-Shafi'i / Ahmad ibn Hanbal themselves, if recorded)?
   - How did the position evolve across subsequent generations? Name the key jurists and their contributions.
   - Were there pivotal turning points — a major jurist who reversed or refined the ruling, a historical event that forced reconsideration?
   - What is the current scholarly consensus within the school, and when did it stabilize?
4. **Internal Dissent** — focus on how internal debate evolved over time, not just the current minority position.

## Quality Signals
- Specific jurists are named at each generational stage — not "later scholars" but "Al-Marghinani in the 12th century held that..."
- The reader understands *why* the ruling evolved, not just *that* it did
- Historical context (political, social, geographic) is connected to juristic development where relevant
- Citations are marked (VERIFIED) or (UNCERTAIN) — historical claims are especially prone to fabrication

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT write a generic ruling card with a paragraph of history appended at the end
- DO NOT assert historical claims without attribution — "scholars in the Abbasid period" is not specific enough
- DO NOT compress centuries of development into a single sentence
- DO NOT fabricate historical citations — if the historical record is unclear, say so explicitly
