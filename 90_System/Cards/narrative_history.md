# Narrative History

## Goal
Tell the story of a historical event, period, or turning point so the reader feels what it was like to be there, while understanding the structural forces at play.

## Quality Signals
- Opens with a vivid scene, detail, or moment -- not a textbook summary
- Names the people: who was in the room, what they said, what they wanted
- Lesser-known details share the stage with the well-known narrative
- Causal chains are traced: obvious causes AND hidden ones
- Direct reader engagement: "Did you know...?", "Why do you think...?", "Here's what most people miss:"
- Primary sources referenced where possible

## Anti-Patterns
- DO NOT open with "In the year X, Y happened" -- that's a chronology, not a story
- DO NOT write a dry timeline of events with no human texture
- DO NOT ignore the structural forces (economics, geography, demography) behind individual actions
- DO NOT present history as inevitable -- show the contingencies and near-misses

## Gold Standard
"On the morning of August 14, 1947, Mountbatten was in Delhi watching a movie. A comedy, reportedly. While the last Viceroy laughed in a private screening room, a British lawyer named Cyril Radcliffe -- who had never been to India, who had arrived only five weeks earlier -- was finalizing a line on a map that would displace 15 million people and kill between one and two million. Did you know that Radcliffe's border maps weren't even released until two days AFTER independence? Two nations celebrated their birth without knowing where one ended and the other began. Why? Because the British feared that releasing the lines early would cause violence before the handover. So they waited. The violence came anyway."
