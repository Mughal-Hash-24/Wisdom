## First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) Scheduling Architecture
First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) is a non-preemptive scheduling algorithm that manages the CPU by allocating it to processes strictly according to their arrival sequence in the ready queue. Internally, the Operating System implements this using a First-In-First-Out (FIFO) queue data structure. When a process transitions from the New to the Ready state, its Process Control Block (PCB) is appended to the tail of the queue. The CPU schedulerâ€”specifically the dispatcherâ€”is triggered whenever the CPU becomes idle, selecting the PCB residing at the head of the queue. Because the algorithm is non-preemptive, once a process gains control of the CPU, it retains execution until it either terminates or requests an I/O operation, regardless of the arrival of higher-priority or shorter tasks.

## Mechanical Analogy: The Single-Lane Toll Booth
Think of FCFS as a single-lane toll booth on a narrow bridge. Vehicles (processes) arrive in a single line. The booth operator (CPU) services the vehicle at the front of the line. Even if a high-speed ambulance (urgent process) or a small motorcycle (short process) arrives behind a heavy, slow-moving semi-truck (long process), they must wait until the truck has fully cleared the booth. The system has no "passing lane" (preemption), and the service order is governed solely by the physical sequence of arrival.

## Implementation and Quantitative Analysis
The logic for FCFS is mathematically straightforward but highly sensitive to the arrival order and burst times ($BT$).

**Pseudocode Logic:**
```c
// When process P arrives at time T
ReadyQueue.Enqueue(P);

// Scheduler Loop
while(true) {
    if (CPU.isIdle() && !ReadyQueue.isEmpty()) {
        Process current = ReadyQueue.Dequeue();
        CPU.Execute(current); // Runs until termination or I/O
    }
}
```

### The Convoy Effect
The primary failure mode of FCFS is the **Convoy Effect**, which occurs when a CPU-bound process with a long burst time precedes several I/O-bound processes with short burst times. The short processes wait in the ready queue for the long process to release the CPU, leading to low CPU and device utilization.

**Example Calculation:**
Consider three processes arriving at $T=0$: $P_1$ ($BT=24$), $P_2$ ($BT=3$), and $P_3$ ($BT=3$).
- **Order $P_1, P_2, P_3$:**
  - Waiting times: $P_1=0; P_2=24; P_3=27$
  - Average Waiting Time: $(0 + 24 + 27) / 3 = 17 \text{ms}$
- **Order $P_2, P_3, P_1$:**
  - Waiting times: $P_2=0; P_3=3; P_1=6$
  - Average Waiting Time: $(0 + 3 + 6) / 3 = 3 \text{ms}$

This stark difference in performance highlights that while FCFS is easy to implement and carries low overhead (no complex priority calculations), its throughput and average turnaround time are highly volatile and generally suboptimal for time-sharing systems.

## FCFS Logic and Mechanism

## FIFO Queuing Logic and State Transitions
First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) is a non-preemptive scheduling discipline that maps the temporal order of process arrivals directly to the execution sequence on the CPU. The operational core of FCFS is a First-In, First-Out (FIFO) queue. When a process enters the `Ready` state, its Process Control Block (PCB) is appended to the tail of this queue. The CPU scheduler (dispatcher) always selects the PCB at the head of the queue for execution.

Because FCFS is non-preemptive, once a process captures the CPU (transitions from `Ready` to `Running`), it retains control until it either terminates or voluntarily initiates an I/O request. In a pure FCFS model, even if a high-priority or shorter process arrives, it must wait behind all previously arrived processes, regardless of their relative importance or execution length.

### The Dispatcher Mechanism
1. **Arrival**: At time $t$, process $P_i$ arrives. If the CPU is idle, $P_i$ is dispatched immediately.
2. **Enqueue**: If the CPU is busy, $P_i$ is placed at the back of the FIFO queue.
3. **Execution**: The CPU executes the process at the head of the queue until completion ($t + BurstTime$).
4. **Dequeue**: Upon termination, the scheduler removes the head element and dispatches the next process in the queue.

## Mathematical Modeling of Execution
The performance of FCFS is entirely dependent on the Arrival Time ($AT$) and Burst Time ($BT$). We track several metrics to evaluate the schedule:

- **Completion Time ($CT$):** The time at which the process finishes execution.
- **Turnaround Time ($TAT$):** The total time spent in the system. $TAT = CT - AT$.
- **Waiting Time ($WT$):** The time spent waiting in the ready queue. $WT = TAT - BT$.

### Pseudocode Implementation
```python
def fcfs_schedule(processes):
    # processes is a list of objects with arrival_time and burst_time
    processes.sort(key=lambda x: x.arrival_time)
    
    current_time = 0
    for p in processes:
        if current_time < p.arrival_time:
            current_time = p.arrival_time
        
        p.start_time = current_time
        p.completion_time = p.start_time + p.burst_time
        p.waiting_time = p.start_time - p.arrival_time
        p.turnaround_time = p.completion_time - p.arrival_time
        
        current_time = p.completion_time
    return processes
```

## The Convoy Effect: A System-Level Performance Bottleneck
The primary failure mode of FCFS is the **Convoy Effect**. This occurs when a CPU-bound process (one with a very large $BT$) arrives just before several I/O-bound processes (those with very small $BT$). 

Imagine a single-lane bridge (the CPU) and a heavy, slow-moving tractor (the long process) followed by ten sports cars (the short processes). Even though the sports cars could clear the bridge in seconds, they are forced to crawl at the speed of the tractor. In an OS context, this results in:
- **Underutilization of I/O devices**: I/O-bound processes sit idle in the ready queue while the CPU-bound process grinds away.
- **High Average Waiting Time**: Since $WT$ is cumulative, a single delay at the head of the queue cascades through all subsequent processes.

## Failure Modes and Constraints
- **Response Time**: FCFS is unsuitable for interactive systems (like GUIs or web servers) because the response time is highly variable and potentially infinite if a previous process enters an infinite loop.
- **Starvation**: Strictly speaking, starvation does not occur in FCFS as long as every process eventually terminates. However, the "perceived starvation" for short processes behind a massive task makes it functionally equivalent in time-sensitive environments.
- **Non-Preemption**: The lack of a timer interrupt to force context switching means the kernel has no mechanism to reclaim the CPU from a runaway user process without external intervention.

## FCFS Non-Preemptive Execution Model

First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) is a non-preemptive scheduling discipline where the dispatcher allocates the CPU to the process at the head of the ready queue and maintains that allocation until the process voluntarily enters a terminated or waiting state. In a non-preemptive context, the OS kernel lacks the mechanismâ€”or the policy authorizationâ€”to trigger a context switch based on external interrupts like timer expirations (quantum exhaustion).

Once a process $P_i$ gains control of the Program Counter (PC), it retains execution rights until:
1. **Termination:** The process completes its execution cycle and calls `exit()`.
2. **I/O Blocking:** The process requests a resource or I/O operation that cannot be immediately satisfied, moving it from the `Running` state to the `Waiting` state.

The scheduler logic is a simple FIFO (First-In, First-Out) queue. It does not re-evaluate priorities or remaining burst times while a process is running.

## Control Flow and State Transitions

In FCFS, the transition from `Running` to `Ready` is impossible without an intervening `Waiting` or `Terminated` state. The CPU scheduler is only invoked when the current process releases the CPU. 

```python

# Conceptual FCFS Scheduler Loop
while True:
    if not ready_queue.is_empty():
        process = ready_queue.dequeue()
        process.state = RUNNING
        
        # CPU is surrendered to the process here. 
        # The scheduler loop effectively pauses until the process returns control.
        execute_on_cpu(process) 
        
        # Control returns to scheduler ONLY after process finishes or blocks
        if process.needs_io:
            process.state = WAITING
            wait_queue.enqueue(process)
        else:
            process.state = TERMINATED
```

The absence of a "timer interrupt handler" that can force a `Running -> Ready` transition defines the non-preemptive nature. The OS remains passive; it does not "take" the CPU back; it "waits" for the CPU to be handed back.

## The Convoy Effect: A Mechanical Breakdown

The primary failure mode of non-preemptive FCFS is the **Convoy Effect**. Consider a single-lane assembly line (the CPU) where a massive, slow-moving heavy-duty engine block (a CPU-bound process) is followed by twenty small spark plugs (I/O-bound processes).

- **The Stall:** The spark plugs require only 1ms of "machine time" to be ready for the next stage, but they are stuck behind the engine block which requires 1000ms.
- **Resource Underutilization:** While the CPU-bound process grinds away, the I/O devices (which the spark plugs would have activated) sit idle.
- **Mathematical Impact:** If Process $P_1$ has a burst time $T_1 = 100$ and $P_2, P_3$ have $T = 1$, the average waiting time is $\frac{0 + 100 + 101}{3} = 67ms$. If the order was reversed, the average waiting time would be $\frac{0 + 1 + 2}{3} = 1ms$.

## Implications for System Responsiveness

System responsiveness is inversely proportional to the maximum CPU burst time of any single process in the queue.

1. **Deterministic but Unfair:** While FCFS is easy to implement and lacks the overhead of frequent context switching, it provides no guarantees for interactive tasks. A user-input thread (low burst) will be blocked by a background calculation (high burst), leading to perceived "freezing."
2. **Turnaround Time Variance:** Turnaround times are highly sensitive to arrival order. A slight shift in arrival time that puts a long process first can double the average wait time for the entire batch.
3. **Throughput vs. Latency:** FCFS can achieve high throughput for batch systems where overhead must be minimized, but its tail latency is catastrophic for real-time or multi-user environments.

In summary, the CPU is not relinquished because the scheduler lacks a preemption trigger. This simplicity transforms the CPU into a "run-to-completion" resource, making the system's responsiveness hostage to the longest process in the ready queue.

## The Convoy Effect: Mechanism and State Dynamics
The Convoy Effect is a phenomenon in non-preemptive First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) scheduling where a single long-running, CPU-bound process occupies the processor, causing a queue of shorter, I/O-bound processes to wait for an extended duration. This results in a "convoy" of processes trailing behind a "slow-moving lead vehicle" (the CPU-intensive task), leading to significant degradation in device utilization and response time.

In FCFS, the dispatcher assigns the CPU to the process at the head of the ready queue. Because the algorithm is non-preemptive, the process retains control until it either terminates or initiates an I/O request. When a CPU-bound process (requiring a long burst) enters the CPU, all other processesÃ¢â‚¬â€regardless of their brevityÃ¢â‚¬â€must wait in the Ready Queue.

## Favoritism Toward CPU-Bound Processes
FCFS inherently favors CPU-bound processes because their execution pattern aligns with the non-preemptive nature of the scheduler. 

1. **CPU-Bound Dominance:** A CPU-bound process executes for its entire burst time without interruption. It remains in the "Running" state for long intervals.
2. **I/O-Bound Starvation:** I/O-bound processes typically have very short CPU bursts followed by long I/O waits. When an I/O-bound process finishes its I/O and returns to the Ready Queue, it finds itself stuck behind the CPU-bound giant. 
3. **The Cycle of Inefficiency:**
   - The CPU-bound process finishes its burst and eventually moves to I/O (or terminates).
   - The I/O-bound processes quickly cycle through the CPU (since their bursts are short) and head back to the I/O queues.
   - The CPU sits idle while waiting for the I/O-bound processes to finish their I/O, or it starts the next CPU-bound process immediately if one is available.
   - The I/O devices remain idle while the CPU-bound process is hogging the processor.

This results in a "pulsing" utilization pattern where either the CPU is 100% busy while I/O devices are idle, or I/O devices are busy while the CPU is underutilized.

## Impact on System Throughput
System throughputÃ¢â‚¬â€the number of processes completed per unit of timeÃ¢â‚¬â€plummets during a convoy effect. 

Consider three processes: $P_1$ (CPU-bound, burst = 100ms), $P_2$ (I/O-bound, burst = 2ms), and $P_3$ (I/O-bound, burst = 2ms).

**FCFS Sequence ($P_1, P_2, P_3$):**
- $P_1$ starts at 0, finishes at 100.
- $P_2$ starts at 100, finishes at 102.
- $P_3$ starts at 102, finishes at 104.
- **Average Waiting Time:** $(0 + 100 + 102) / 3 = 67.33$ ms.

If $P_2$ and $P_3$ had arrived just before $P_1$:
**FCFS Sequence ($P_2, P_3, P_1$):**
- $P_2$ starts at 0, finishes at 2.
- $P_3$ starts at 2, finishes at 4.
- $P_1$ starts at 4, finishes at 104.
- **Average Waiting Time:** $(0 + 2 + 4) / 3 = 2$ ms.

The convoy effect increases the average waiting time and turnaround time, reducing the frequency of process completions (throughput) because the "short" jobs are blocked by the "long" job.

## Mechanical Analogy: The Single-Lane Toll Plaza
Imagine a single-lane highway leading to a toll plaza (the CPU). 
- **CPU-bound processes** are heavy tractor-trailers carrying complex oversized loads that require 10 minutes of paperwork at the window.
- **I/O-bound processes** are motorcycles that take 5 seconds to tap a card and leave.

If a tractor-trailer arrives first, a line of 50 motorcycles forms behind it. Even though the motorcycles could be cleared in minutes, they are held hostage for the full 10 minutes of the trailer's paperwork. While the motorcycles wait, the rest of the highway (I/O devices like disks and network cards) remains empty and underutilized because the vehicles that should be driving on it are stuck at the toll booth.

## Failure Modes and Mitigation
The primary failure mode of FCFS is **Indefinite Blocking** (not true starvation, as the CPU-bound process will eventually finish, but effectively high latency). FCFS fails in interactive systems where response time is critical because a single background calculation can freeze the UI.

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **Preemption (Round Robin):** The most common fix. Give the CPU-bound process a "time slice." When the slice expires, force it back to the queue, allowing I/O-bound processes to sneak in.
- **Shortest Job First (SJF):** Prioritize the motorcycles over the tractor-trailers to minimize average wait time.
- **Multi-level Feedback Queues:** Move CPU-bound processes to lower-priority queues so they don't block high-priority interactive tasks.

## Calculation Mechanics: The Cumulative Delay
Waiting Time ($WT$) in First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) scheduling is the total time a process spends in the ready queue before its execution begins. It is the delta between the time a process arrives ($AT$) and the time it actually starts execution ($ST$).

$$WT_i = ST_i - AT_i$$

In a non-preemptive FCFS system, the Start Time ($ST$) of a process $P_i$ is strictly determined by the completion time of the process immediately preceding it ($P_{i-1}$). If the CPU is idle when $P_i$ arrives, $ST_i = AT_i$, resulting in $WT = 0$. However, if the CPU is busy, $P_i$ must wait for the sum of the remaining burst time of the current process and the full burst times of all processes that arrived before $P_i$ but after the current process started.

## The Sequential Dependency Chain
The fundamental internal logic of FCFS is a **linear dependency chain**. Because the scheduler does not reorder the queue based on process characteristics (like priority or burst length), the Waiting Time of process $P_n$ is a function of the arrival and burst times of $P_0, P_1, \dots, P_{n-1}$.

### Mathematical Recursive Relation
For a set of processes arriving at the same time ($AT = 0$):
- $WT_0 = 0$
- $WT_1 = BT_0$
- $WT_2 = BT_0 + BT_1$
- $WT_n = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} BT_j$

Where $BT$ is the Burst Time. This demonstrates that $WT_n$ is entirely indifferent to $BT_n$. A process requiring 1ms of CPU time will wait exactly as long as a process requiring 1000ms if they occupy the same position in the queue.

### Analogy: The Single-Lane Toll Booth
Consider a single-lane bridge with a toll booth. 
- **Arrival Order:** The sequence in which cars reach the lane.
- **Burst Time:** The time it takes for a driver to find their wallet and pay the toll.
- **Waiting Time:** The time a driver spends idling in line.

If a semi-truck (high $BT$) arrives first, every sports car (low $BT$) behind it is delayed by the truck's processing time. The sports car's speed or urgency (priority) is irrelevant; its delay is a direct accumulation of the "burst times" of every vehicle ahead of it in the physical queue.

## Failure Mode: The Convoy Effect
The primary performance bottleneck in FCFS is the **Convoy Effect**. This occurs when a single CPU-bound process (long burst time) precedes multiple I/O-bound processes (short burst times).

1. **State Transition:** The long process $P_{long}$ occupies the CPU.
2. **Queue Saturation:** Shorter processes $P_1, P_2, \dots, P_n$ arrive and migrate to the Ready Queue.
3. **Internal Delay:** Even though $P_1 \dots P_n$ could finish quickly and move to I/O devices (keeping the system's hardware utilized), they are blocked.
4. **Result:** CPU utilization remains high, but throughput drops and average waiting time spikes drastically because the short processes are forced to inherit the massive burst time of $P_{long}$ into their own waiting time calculation.

## Performance Metrics in FCFS Scheduling

In a First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) scheduling environment, performance is measured by the temporal delta between a process entering the ready queue and its eventual state transitions (start of execution and completion). FCFS is a non-preemptive algorithm where the CPU is allocated to processes in the exact order of their arrival, maintaining a strict FIFO (First-In, First-Out) queue.

## Formulaic Derivations

The derivation of these metrics relies on four primary timestamps for each process $P_i$:
- **Arrival Time ($AT_i$):** The moment the process enters the ready queue.
- **Burst Time ($BT_i$):** The total CPU time required by the process.
- **Completion Time ($CT_i$):** The moment the process finishes execution and exits the system.
- **Start Time ($ST_i$):** The moment the process first gains access to the CPU.

### 1. Turnaround Time (TAT)
Turnaround Time represents the total residence time of a process within the system. It encompasses the time spent waiting in the ready queue plus the time spent executing on the CPU.

$$TAT_i = CT_i - AT_i$$

Alternatively, if waiting time is already known:
$$TAT_i = WT_i + BT_i$$

### 2. Response Time (RT)
Response Time measures the latency between the submission of a request (arrival) and the first response from the system (first allocation of the CPU).

$$RT_i = ST_i - AT_i$$

**Mechanical Note:** In non-preemptive FCFS scheduling, Response Time is mathematically identical to Waiting Time ($WT$), because once a process starts, it does not yield the CPU until completion. Therefore, the "first" time it gets the CPU is also the *only* time it begins its execution phase.

## The Sequential Pipeline: A Mechanical Trace

Think of FCFS as a **Single-Lane Toll Booth**. Cars (processes) arrive at different times. The booth operator (CPU) can only service one car at a time. 
- **TAT** is the total time you spend in the vicinity of the toll plaza (from joining the line to passing the gate).
- **RT** is the time you spend idling in the line before the collector finally says "Hello" and takes your money.

### Internal Calculation Flow
The OS derives these values by maintaining a global clock ($T$).

1. **Wait State:** $T$ advances as the CPU processes $P_{i-1}$.
2. **Allocation:** If $T < AT_i$, the CPU idles until $AT_i$. Then $ST_i = \max(T, AT_i)$.
3. **Execution:** The clock moves to $CT_i = ST_i + BT_i$.
4. **Metric Logging:** 
   - $TAT_i = (ST_i + BT_i) - AT_i$
   - $RT_i = ST_i - AT_i$

### Logic Diagram (Gantt Trace)
| Process | Arrival ($AT$) | Burst ($BT$) | Start ($ST$) | Completion ($CT$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $P_1$ | 0 | 5 | 0 | 5 |
| $P_2$ | 2 | 3 | 5 | 8 |
| $P_3$ | 4 | 1 | 8 | 9 |

- **$P_2$ TAT:** $8 - 2 = 6$
- **$P_2$ RT:** $5 - 2 = 3$

## Failure Modes: The Convoy Effect

The primary failure mode of FCFS metrics is the **Convoy Effect**. This occurs when a CPU-bound process with a massive $BT$ arrives just before several I/O-bound processes with tiny $BTs$.

- **Symptom:** The average $RT$ and $TAT$ skyrocket for all subsequent processes.
- **Mechanism:** Because FCFS lacks preemption, the short processes are blocked behind the "heavy" process (the head of the convoy). Even if a process only needs 1ms of CPU time, if it arrives at $T=1$ behind a process that needs 100ms and arrived at $T=0$, its $RT$ will be $99ms$. 

In this scenario, the system's throughput drops effectively to the service rate of the slowest process, leading to poor resource utilization for I/O devices which sit idle while the "convoy leader" holds the CPU.

First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) is a "naive postal clerk" that respects the queue but ignores the parcel size. ## The Mechanics of Divergence
The divergence between the worst and best cases in FCFS is driven by **Head-of-Line (HoL) Blocking**. 

In the **Worst-Case**, a CPU-intensive process occupies the processor for a long duration. During this time, multiple short processes (often I/O-bound) accumulate in the ready queue. These short processes could have finished their CPU burst and moved to I/O devices, keeping the hardware busy. Instead, they wait, and the I/O devices sit idle. This is the "Convoy Effect"Ã¢â‚¬â€a line of fast vehicles stuck behind a slow-moving tractor on a one-lane highway.

In the **Best-Case**, the short processes finish quickly, vacate the CPU, and potentially move to I/O, allowing the long process to eventually take the CPU without delaying others. The mathematical result is a drastically lower Average Waiting Time.

## Switching Criteria
While FCFS is the simplest to implement, it must be abandoned in favor of Preemptive Round Robin or SJF when:
1.  **Burst Variance > 50%:** If your workload consists of a mix of micro-services (short bursts) and batch processing (long bursts).
2.  **I/O Idle Time:** If telemetry shows I/O devices are frequently idle while the Ready Queue is non-empty.
3.  **SLA Requirements:** If interactive user responsiveness is required, as FCFS provides no guarantee for maximum response time.

First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) is a non-preemptive scheduling algorithm where the CPU is allocated to processes in the exact order of their arrival in the Ready Queue. It is implemented using a First-In, First-Out (FIFO) queue data structure. Once a process gains control of the CPU, it holds it until it either terminates or requests I/O.

## Mechanism: The FIFO Pipeline
The scheduler treats the Ready Queue as a strict linear pipe.
1. **Enqueue:** When a process enters the `Ready` state, its Process Control Block (PCB) is linked to the tail of the queue.
2. **Dispatch:** The CPU scheduler selects the PCB at the head of the queue.
3. **Execution:** The process runs to completion (non-preemptive).
4. **Dequeue:** Upon termination, the process is removed; the scheduler then dispatches the next PCB in line.

**Analogy: The Single-Teller Bank**
Imagine a bank with exactly one teller and no "express lanes." Customers (processes) stand in a single line. The teller (CPU) serves the person at the front until their entire transaction (Burst Time) is finished. Even if the first person has a 30-minute task and the ten people behind them have 1-minute tasks, everyone must wait.

## Step-by-Step Execution Trace
We will trace the following process set:

| Process ID | Arrival Time (AT) | Burst Time (BT) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| P1 | 0 | 5 |
| P2 | 1 | 3 |
| P3 | 2 | 8 |
| P4 | 6 | 2 |

### Detailed Execution Flow
- **t = 0:** P1 arrives and is immediately enqueued. Since the CPU is idle, P1 is dispatched.
    - *Ready Queue:* [Empty] | *Executing:* P1
- **t = 1:** P2 arrives. P1 is still executing (non-preemptive). P2 enters the Ready Queue.
    - *Ready Queue:* [P2] | *Executing:* P1
- **t = 2:** P3 arrives. P3 is added to the tail of the queue.
    - *Ready Queue:* [P2, P3] | *Executing:* P1
- **t = 5:** P1 completes execution. The scheduler pulls the head of the queue (P2).
    - *Ready Queue:* [P3] | *Executing:* P2
- **t = 6:** P4 arrives and is added to the queue.
    - *Ready Queue:* [P3, P4] | *Executing:* P2
- **t = 8:** P2 completes. The scheduler dispatches P3.
    - *Ready Queue:* [P4] | *Executing:* P3
- **t = 16:** P3 completes. The scheduler dispatches P4.
    - *Ready Queue:* [Empty] | *Executing:* P4
- **t = 18:** P4 completes. CPU becomes idle.

## Gantt Chart
The timeline of CPU occupancy (visualizing the "Gaunt" chart):

```text
0      5      8               16     18
+------+------+---------------+------+
|  P1  |  P2  |       P3      |  P4  |
+------+------+---------------+------+
```

## Calculation Table
To evaluate performance, we calculate Completion Time (CT), Turnaround Time (TAT = CT - AT), and Waiting Time (WT = TAT - BT).

| PID | AT | BT | CT | TAT | WT |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| P1 | 0 | 5 | 5 | 5 | 0 |
| P2 | 1 | 3 | 8 | 7 | 4 |
| P3 | 2 | 8 | 16 | 14 | 6 |
| P4 | 6 | 2 | 18 | 12 | 10 |
| **Total** | | | | **38** | **20** |

- **Average Turnaround Time:** 38 / 4 = **9.5 units**
- **Average Waiting Time:** 20 / 4 = **5.0 units**

## Failure Modes: The Convoy Effect
The primary inefficiency of FCFS is the **Convoy Effect**. This occurs when a CPU-bound process (long burst time) arrives before several I/O-bound processes (short burst times). 

In the example above, if P1 had a Burst Time of 100, P2, P3, and P4 would all be blocked for 100 units regardless of how short their tasks were. This leads to low CPU and device utilization, as the I/O-bound processes stay in the Ready Queue instead of cycling through I/O devices, effectively "starving" the system's throughput because of a single heavy process at the front of the line.
