# Scheduling Misc
## Issues
> **Seed:** "@expand Explain in detail each and every issue we encounter during process scheduling and how do we fix em"

Process scheduling is the mechanism by which the OS kernel allocates CPU time-slices to active threads or processes, aiming to maximize throughput, minimize latency, and ensure fairness. Failure in scheduling logic leads to systemic pathologies where the CPU cycles are wasted or specific processes are denied execution indefinitely.

## 1. Starvation (Indefinite Blocking)
Starvation occurs in priority-based scheduling algorithms when a low-priority process is perpetually bypassed by a steady stream of higher-priority processes. In a strictly preemptive priority system, a low-priority process may reside in the "Ready" queue indefinitely if the arrival rate of high-priority tasks exceeds the CPU's processing capacity for those tasks.

**The Fix: Aging**
Aging is a technique that gradually increases the priority of processes that have waited in the ready queue for a long time. By incrementing the priority at fixed intervals, even the lowest-priority process will eventually ascend to the highest-priority tier.

```python
def on_clock_tick(ready_queue):
    for process in ready_queue:
        process.wait_time += 1
        if process.wait_time > THRESHOLD:
            process.dynamic_priority += 1
            process.wait_time = 0 # Reset interval
```

## 2. Priority Inversion
Priority inversion is a scenario where a high-priority process ($P_H$) is indirectly preempted by a medium-priority process ($P_M$), because $P_H$ is waiting for a resource held by a low-priority process ($P_L$). While $P_L$ holds the resource, $P_M$ (which does not need the resource) preempts $P_L$ due to its higher priority. Consequently, $P_H$ is blocked by $P_M$, effectively inverting the priority logic.

**The Fix: Priority Inheritance Protocol (PIP)**
The kernel implements priority inheritance: when $P_H$ blocks on a resource held by $P_L$, $P_L$ temporarily inherits the priority of $P_H$. This ensures that $P_L$ can complete its task and release the resource without being preempted by $P_M$. Once the resource is released, $P_L$ reverts to its original priority.

## 3. The Convoy Effect
The Convoy Effect is primarily a pathology of First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) scheduling. It occurs when a single CPU-bound process (the "heavy truck") occupies the processor for a long duration, while a "convoy" of I/O-bound processes (the "faster cars") wait behind it. These I/O-bound processes could finish quickly and move back to their I/O devices, keeping the hardware busy, but instead, they sit idle in the Ready queue, leading to low device utilization and poor responsiveness.

**The Fix: Preemptive Round Robin or MLFQ**
The system introduces a "Time Quantum" (time-slice). If a process exceeds its quantum, it is forcibly preempted and moved to the back of the queue. Multilevel Feedback Queues (MLFQ) further refine this by dropping the priority of CPU-bound processes that exhaust their quantum, allowing I/O-bound processes to "jump" to the front of higher-priority queues.

## 4. Context Switch Overhead (Thrashing)
Every time the scheduler switches from Process A to Process B, the kernel must save the CPU registers, program counter, and stack pointer of A, and load those of B. This "Context Switch" consumes CPU cycles that perform no "useful" work for the user. If the time quantum is too small, the system spends more time switching than executing code (thrashing).

**The Fix: Quantum Tuning**
The rule of thumb is that the time quantum should be significantly larger than the context switch time.
- If $T_{switch}$ is 10 microseconds and $T_{quantum}$ is 100 microseconds, overhead is 10%.
- If $T_{quantum}$ is 10 milliseconds, overhead drops to 0.1%.
Schedulers must balance this against responsiveness; larger quanta increase the wait time for interactive processes.

## 5. Fairness vs. Real-Time Constraints
In general-purpose scheduling, fairness is a goal (every process gets a turn). In Real-Time Operating Systems (RTOS), fairness is discarded in favor of "Determinism." A real-time task must meet its deadline regardless of how many other processes are waiting.

**The Fix: Rate-Monotonic or Earliest Deadline First (EDF)**
- **Rate-Monotonic Scheduling (RMS):** Assigns priorities based on the periodicity of tasks (shorter period = higher priority).
- **Earliest Deadline First (EDF):** A dynamic algorithm that prioritizes whichever task is closest to its deadline ($d_i$).

$$P_i = \max(d_{all}) - d_i$$

## 6. Livelock
Unlike deadlock where processes are frozen, livelock occurs when processes continuously change their state in response to each other without making progress. In scheduling, this often happens in "Busy-Wait" scenarios where two threads are attempting to yield to each other in a loop, consuming 100% CPU without advancing.

**The Fix: Backoff Algorithms and Yielding**
Instead of immediate retry, processes implement an exponential backoff or use kernel-level primitives (mutexes/semaphores) that put the process into a "Waiting" state (blocked), removing it from the scheduler's consideration until the resource is actually available.
## Goals
> **Seed:** "Explain in detail the goals of process scheduling especially in the context of optimization"

Process scheduling is the set of mechanisms and policies used by the Operating System (OS) to allocate CPU time to active processes. Its primary internal objective is to maximize the utilization of the processor while ensuring that specific performance constraints—such as latency and throughput—are met according to the system's operational context (e.g., real-time, interactive, or batch).

## The Optimization Vectors: Primary Metrics

In process scheduling, optimization is a multi-objective problem. The OS attempts to satisfy five primary mathematical criteria:

1.  **CPU Utilization ($U$):** The fraction of time the CPU is busy executing user or system code. The goal is to keep $U \approx 100\%$ in high-load systems.
2.  **Throughput ($T$):** The number of processes that complete their execution per unit of time ($T = \frac{N}{\Delta t}$). This is the "factory output" metric.
3.  **Turnaround Time ($TAT$):** The interval from the time of submission of a process to the time of completion.
    *   $TAT = T_{completion} - T_{submission}$
4.  **Waiting Time ($WT$):** The sum of the periods spent waiting in the ready queue.
    *   $WT = TAT - \text{Burst Time}$
5.  **Response Time ($RT$):** The time from the submission of a request until the first response is produced. This is critical for interactive systems where the user needs immediate feedback.

## The Mechanical Analogy: The Single-Arm Assembly Line

Imagine a high-speed robotic assembly arm (the CPU) capable of performing tasks on various products (processes).
*   **Throughput optimization** is like configuring the line to finish as many products as possible in an hour, regardless of how long any single product stays on the belt.
*   **Response Time optimization** is like ensuring that as soon as a new product arrives, the arm touches it immediately, even if only for a millisecond, to acknowledge it.
*   **Context Switching** is the "retooling" time required for the arm to move from one product type to another. If the scheduler switches too often (high frequency), the "retooling" overhead eats into the actual production time, lowering CPU utilization.

## Trade-offs and Optimization Conflicts

The scheduler cannot optimize all metrics simultaneously; they are often inversely proportional.

*   **Fairness vs. Throughput:** A "Fair" scheduler (like Round Robin) gives every process an equal slice of time ($q$). While this minimizes Response Time, it increases the total Context Switch overhead, which reduces overall Throughput compared to a "Greedy" scheduler (like First-Come, First-Served) that minimizes retooling.
*   **Interactive vs. Batch:** In an interactive environment (a PC), the scheduler prioritizes **Response Time**. In a batch environment (a scientific simulation cluster), the scheduler prioritizes **Throughput** and **CPU Utilization**.

### Priority Inversion and Starvation

Optimization logic often introduces failure modes. In a **Priority Scheduling** system, the goal is to ensure critical tasks run first. However, this leads to **Starvation**, where a low-priority process never receives CPU time because a steady stream of high-priority processes keeps the CPU occupied.

The internal fix is **Aging**:
```pseudo
// Logic for an Aging Mechanism to prevent Starvation
On TimerInterrupt(interval):
    for each process p in ReadyQueue:
        if p.priority < MAX_PRIORITY:
            p.wait_time += interval
            if p.wait_time > AGING_THRESHOLD:
                p.priority += 1  // Incrementally boost priority
                p.wait_time = 0
```

## Internal Mechanism: The Dispatcher

The goals of the scheduler are realized by the **Dispatcher**. While the scheduler *decides* which process is next, the dispatcher *implements* that decision. The time it takes for the dispatcher to stop one process and start another is known as **Dispatch Latency**.

The optimization of Dispatch Latency involves:
1.  **Saving the State:** Writing the current Program Counter (PC) and CPU registers into the Process Control Block (PCB).
2.  **Updating States:** Moving the old process to the 'Ready' or 'Waiting' state and the new process to the 'Running' state.
3.  **Restoring the State:** Loading the registers of the next process from its PCB and jumping to its saved PC.

If the Dispatch Latency becomes a significant percentage of the CPU time, the system enters a state of **Thrashing**, where the OS spends more energy managing processes than the processes spend doing work. Optimization here requires balancing the time quantum ($q$) against the cost of the context switch ($s$). Efficient schedulers typically aim for $q \gg s$.
