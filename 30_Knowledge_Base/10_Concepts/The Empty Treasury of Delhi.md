# The Empty Treasury of Delhi

In the spring of 1740, Siraj-ud-Din Ali Khan, known to history as Khan-e Arzu, sat in his Delhi courtyard amidst a quiet that felt heavier than usual. Just months earlier, the Persian warlord Nader Shah had marched into the city, slaughtered thirty thousand citizens, and stripped the imperial treasury bare. The Peacock Throne was gone.

The gold coins that once drew ambitious poets from Isfahan and Shiraz to Delhi had vanished into the saddlebags of retreating soldiers. For centuries, the Mughal elite had looked north to Iran for their cultural cues, paying premium prices for "pure" Persian verse. Now, the money was gone. The poets of Iran stopped coming. How does a court maintain its aura of aristocratic refinement when it can no longer afford its imported language?

The answer lay in a language born on the streets and in the military camps surrounding the Red Fort. For decades, the economic foundations of the empire had been crumbling under the weight of Aurangzeb’s endless military campaigns in the Deccan. Mansabdars—the military aristocrats—found themselves holding land grants that yielded no revenue. The administrative machine was gridlocked.

When the treasury dried up, the patron-client networks that sustained the elite disintegrated. Why do you think the local nobility suddenly stopped hiring foreign tutors for their children? They simply could not pay them. The domestic elite had to find cultural value in what they already possessed: their own spoken tongue.
