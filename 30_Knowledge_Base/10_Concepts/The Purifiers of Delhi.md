# The Purifiers of Delhi

This realization changed the script of history. The language adopted the Perso-Arabic *Nasta'līq* script, a calligraphic style developed in fourteenth-century Persia. This was not merely a choice of aesthetics; it was a political alignment. Writing Hindavi in the script of the Quran and the Persian court tied the language directly to the ruling class's source of legitimacy. It also meant that Arabic loanwords, which came via Persian, could be spelled in their original form, even if the local tongue could not pronounce the guttural sounds. Thus, the Arabic *khalaq* (creation) kept its spelling, but was pronounced with a simple soft 'k' or a frictioned 'kh' depending on the speaker's education.

Why did the elite of Delhi begin systematically weeding out their own native words in the eighteenth century? As the Mughal Empire began to fracture after the death of Aurangzeb, the Delhi elite felt their political dominance slipping. In response, they sought to define their cultural identity. Scholars like Khan-e-Arzu, one of the greatest linguists of his age, led a movement to "purify" the language. They systematically replaced local Sanskrit-derived words (*tadbhava*) with high Persian and Arabic vocabulary. If a local word sounded too rustic, it was discarded. This process created a sharp class divide, separating the highly Persianized *Rekhta* of the court from the *Khari Boli of the countryside, setting the stage for the modern linguistic divide of the subcontinent.
