# The Southern Crucible: Dakhni and the Royal Ghazal

But how did a language of the Delhi fields become the language of Deccan kings? When Tughluq’s forced migrants settled in the Deccan, their northern dialect fused with local Marathi, Telugu, and Kannada influences, creating Dakhni. In the courts of Golconda and Bijapur, kings like Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah—who founded the city of Hyderabad—wrote poetry in this local tongue. They wrote not in the traditional Sanskrit meters, but in the Persian *ghazal* form, adopting its conventions of the beloved, the tavern, and the nightingale. In his poetry, Qutb Shah openly celebrated this hybrid identity. He wrote of local monsoon rains and Hindu festivals while using the poetic structures of Shiraz and Isfahan.

Consider the advice that changed the course of northern literature. In the year 1700, the Dakhni poet Wali Gujarati traveled to Delhi. His simple, musical verses caused a sensation. The northern elite, who had looked down on Hindavi as a vulgar tongue fit only for the marketplace, suddenly realized that this vernacular could carry the weight of high Persian aesthetics. Wali met with the Delhi Sufi scholar Shah Gulshan, who gave him a crucial piece of advice: "Use these Dakhni idioms in the Delhi style, and translate Persian themes into your vernacular." This advice sparked a revolution. The Delhi poets abandoned Persian for their own language, which they called Rekhta, meaning "the mixed tongue."
