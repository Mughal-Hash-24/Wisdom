# The Forced Caravan: A Dialect Transplanted

In the winter of 1327, a strange and bitter caravan wound its way south from Delhi. Sultan Muhammad bin Tughluq had commanded the entire population of the imperial capital to march seven hundred miles to his new stronghold in the Deccan, Daulatabad. Among the elite, the soldiers, and the common traders was a shared vernacular—a local speech they called Hindavi. They spoke it to their servants, their cooks, and the market merchants. It was the speech of the Delhi bazaar, born of the daily friction between local Indo-Aryan speakers and the Persian-speaking administrations of the late twelfth century. This forced march did not just move people. It transplanted a dialect, setting off a chain reaction that would shape the literary landscape of South Asia for the next five hundred years.
