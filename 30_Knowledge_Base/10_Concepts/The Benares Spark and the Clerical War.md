# The Benares Spark and the Clerical War

Why did a bureaucratic system that worked for three decades suddenly crack in Benares? In 1867, a group of Hindu elites presented a petition demanding that the government replace Urdu with Hindi, written in the Devanagari script. On the surface, this was a debate about communication and access for the illiterate majority. Beneath the surface, it was an economic war. If Devanagari became the standard, the highly skilled Muslim and Kayastha clerks would become illiterate overnight. Their years of specialized education would be worthless. Conversely, Brahmins and other high-caste Hindus, who were fluent in Sanskrit and Devanagari but excluded from the courts, would suddenly find the doors of government employment flung wide open.

British administrators watched this brewing storm with a mixture of confusion and pragmatic calculation. They faced a bureaucratic headache. Scribes in local courts frequently wrote in a style so cramped and personalized that even English judges could not decipher the records. Yet, the British could not simply sweep away the existing administrative class without bringing local government to a screeching halt. Sir William Muir, the Lieutenant-Governor, hesitated. He knew that changing the script would alienate the Muslim elite, whose loyalty was already fragile after the rebellion of 1857. The colonial state sought administrative efficiency, but its primary constraint was maintaining political stability among its subjects.

The immediate aftermath of the Benares petition did not bring a swift policy change, but it did ignite a fierce pamphlet war. Scribes did not argue about poetry or religion; they argued about jobs, salaries, and pensions. Muslim intellectuals argued that the Persian script was faster to write, making it ideal for the rapid pace of court proceedings. Hindi advocates countered that Devanagari was phonetic and transparent, preventing corrupt clerks from altering legal records to cheat illiterate peasants. What began as a dispute over professional qualifications quickly transformed. To rally support, both sides appealed to a broader public, casting the struggle not as a labor dispute, but as a defense of religious heritage.
