# The Scribes and the Typecasters

Before the machine, the pen was king. In the early nineteenth century, the administrative heart of British India ran on Urdu written in the Nastaliq script. It was a fluid, cursive hand, imported from Persia and nurtured in Mughal courts. Hindus and Muslims alike studied it to gain employment in tax offices and courts. Scribes, known as katibs, held immense social power. They did not just copy texts; they curated the visual beauty of literature. But this scribal culture was slow, expensive, and fragile.

Then came the typefounders. The British colonial state demanded speed, uniformity, and cheapness. Moveable type—individual metal blocks for each letter—had conquered Europe. It worked because European letters stood side by side like soldiers. Devanagari, the script of Sanskrit and Hindi, could be adapted to this grid. Despite its conjunct consonants, it sat on a horizontal baseline. Typecasters could slice it into discrete metal blocks. But Nastaliq resisted.
