# Game Theory of Cartels

### The Logic of the Cheat
Why does a cartel fail? It isn't for lack of profit. It is because the math of individual greed always outruns the math of collective stability. Every member of a price-fixing ring knows that if everyone holds the line, everyone gets rich. But they also know that if they cheat just a little—selling a few extra units at the inflated price—they get even richer. 

The players are the firms. The strategies are binary: Limit or Flood. The payoffs follow the grim geometry of the Prisoners' Dilemma. If Firm A and Firm B both limit supply, they split a $100 million monopoly profit. If Firm A cheats while Firm B stays loyal, Firm A grabs $80 million for itself while Firm B's share craters to $20 million. The dominant strategy is to cheat. Every time. Even when the result is a $40 million profit for both in a competitive market.

### The Monitoring Tax
Trust is a sucker’s bet in a market without a central enforcer. In a one-shot game, the cartel collapses before the first meeting ends. To survive, cartels rely on repeated games and "trigger" strategies—threatening to flood the market forever if anyone cheats once. This sounds stable on paper. It isn't in practice. 

Monitoring is the hidden tax on cooperation. In the real world, prices are noisy. If the market price drops, did Firm A cheat on its quota, or did consumer demand simply fall? In the fog of the market, every price dip looks like a betrayal. The "loyal" members face a choice: ignore the dip and risk being the "sucker," or retaliate and destroy the cartel. Most choose to shoot first.

### The Swing Producer’s Burden
Who pays for the cartel’s survival? Usually, the largest member. In OPEC, Saudi Arabia historically acted as the "swing producer," cutting its own production to offset the cheating of smaller members like Venezuela or Nigeria. The big player sacrifices its own market share to keep the price floor intact. 

But this sacrifice has a limit. Eventually, the cost of being the only honest player becomes too high. The leader realizes it is subsidizing its competitors' growth. At that point, the leader floods the market to punish the cheaters, as seen in the 2014 and 2020 oil price wars. The cartel doesn't fail because they forgot how to cooperate. It fails because the incentive to be a "free rider" is more powerful than the fear of the crash.

### Innovation as the Ultimate Cheat
Even if a cartel manages to hold its members in line, the high prices they maintain create a massive incentive for outsiders. Who benefits from a steel cartel? The aluminum manufacturer. By keeping prices artificially high, the cartel acts as a venture capital fund for its own replacement. 

They provide the "price umbrella" that allows new, more efficient competitors to enter the market. The monopoly outcome is a signal to every engineer and entrepreneur on the planet: "There is a massive profit margin here if you can find a workaround." The collapse isn't always an internal betrayal. Sometimes, it is just the market routeing around a bottleneck.
