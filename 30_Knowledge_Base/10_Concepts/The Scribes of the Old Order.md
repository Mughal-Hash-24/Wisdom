# The Scribes of the Old Order

Imagine a world where a single stroke of a pen could strip a family of its livelihood. In the dusty, record-filled offices of the nineteenth-century United Provinces, the ink-stained fingers of Muslim and Kayastha clerks held a quiet monopoly. For decades, they had translated the language of rulers into the daily business of local administration. They wrote in Shikasta, a cursive, rapid form of the Persian script. To write Shikasta was to possess a valuable guild secret, passed down from father to son, securing jobs in the local courts and administrative offices. 

This monopoly rested on a strategic British decision made in 1837. Desperate to replace Persian as the official language of local administration with something the common people understood, the East India Company chose Urdu in the North-Western Provinces. It was a compromise. While the grammar was local, the script remained Persian, written in the elegant Nastaliq or the swift Shikasta. The shift did not democratize the bureaucracy. Instead, it preserved the privileges of the traditional literati class. Kayasthas, a Hindu caste of scribes, and elite Muslims retained their tight grip on administrative positions because they alone possessed the linguistic training.
