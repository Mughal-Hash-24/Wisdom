# The Balance Sheet of Literacy and Identity

What happens when one language runs on metal type while another is bound to stone? The colonial government wanted to educate millions of rural children as cheaply as possible. Hindi textbooks printed in Devanagari moveable type were cheap to produce, easy to revise, and quick to distribute. Urdu textbooks, relying on the slow craft of lithography, grew relatively expensive. School inspectors in the North-Western Provinces noticed the difference. They began pushing Hindi as the language of primary education. A new generation of rural boys grew up literate in Nagri, completely alienated from the Nastaliq administration.

The economics of the press soon dictated the politics of the street. Hindi newspaper editors, employing moveable type, could run daily editions with late-breaking news, easily resetting pages as events unfolded. Urdu editors had to wait for the katib to finish writing the entire sheet before they could even think of printing. Profit margins for Urdu papers shrank. To survive, Urdu presses had to appeal to a loyal, impassioned readership. They leaned into cultural defense, portraying the defense of the Nastaliq script as a defense of Islam itself. Hindi advocates responded in kind, branding Devanagari as the sacred script of the Hindus, free from foreign contagion.

By the time the century closed, the technical limitations of the printing press had hardened into political identities. The Nagri Resolution of 1900, which allowed Devanagari equal status in the courts of the United Provinces, was seen by Urdu elites as a mortal blow. It was not just a linguistic shift; it was an economic eviction. Scribes lost their livelihoods, Urdu presses went bankrupt, and the administrative monopoly of the old Muslim gentry shattered. The mechanical incompatibility of Nastaliq with lead type did not just change how books were made. It dismantled a syncretic administrative elite and laid the structural tracks for the partition of a subcontinent.
