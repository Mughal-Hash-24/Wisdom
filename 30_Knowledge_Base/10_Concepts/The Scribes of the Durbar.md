# The Scribes of the Durbar

In the dusty heat of 1850s Delhi, Mirza Asadullah Khan Ghalib dipped his reed pen into black ink. He was not writing to a Muslim nobleman or a court cleric. He was writing to Munshi Har Gopal 'Tafta', a Hindu of the Kayastha scribal caste. Ghalib addressed Tafta with a warmth reserved for intellectual equals, calling him a master of Urdu verse. To Ghalib, Urdu was not the property of a mosque or a specific creed. It was the language of the street, the administrative bureau, and the elite salon. Why do you think a language once so shared became a battleground of identity?

Here is what most people miss about the birth of Urdu: it did not emerge from religious conversion, but from the pragmatic realities of state service. In 1582, Emperor Akbar’s finance minister, a Hindu Khatri named Raja Todar Mal, made a decision that changed the linguistic landscape of India. He decreed that all imperial administrative records must be kept in Persian, replacing local vernaculars. For Hindu scribal castes like the Kayasthas, Khatris, and Kashmiri Pandits, learning the ruler’s tongue was not a religious statement. It was a matter of survival. It was their bread and butter.

Over the next two centuries, these Hindu groups mastered Persian grammar, administration, and poetry. They became the backbone of the Mughal empire and the regional kingdoms that followed. When Persian began to yield to a highly Persianized vernacular known as Rekhta, Hindustani, or Urdu in the late eighteenth century, these elites did not abandon their desks. They simply brought their Persianized vocabulary to the local grammar of Delhi and Lucknow.

Did you know that the first major history of Urdu literature was written by a Kashmiri Pandit? Ram Babu Saksena’s work tracked how deeply embedded Hindu writers were in the very fabric of the language. In the nineteenth century, Pandit Daya Shankar Naseem wrote *Gulzar-e-Naseem*, a mathnawi poem that stands as one of the twin peaks of classical Urdu narrative verse. The Kashmiri Pandit community of Lucknow and Allahabad lived and breathed Urdu. For them, Urdu was the language of domestic life, marriages, and letters. They spoke it at home, wrote it in their offices, and shaped its literary conventions.

Class status, not religious belief, dictated this literacy. A poor Muslim peasant in Bengal spoke Bengali, not Urdu. Meanwhile, a wealthy Kayastha landlord in Lucknow or a Khatri merchant in Lahore wrote his ledger and his love letters in Urdu. The language was a marker of the *sharafat*—the respectable elite. It separated the educated gentry from the unlettered masses, regardless of how they prayed. The British East India Company cemented this in the 1830s. When they abolished Persian as the official language of the courts, they replaced it with Urdu in the north-central provinces. To secure a government job as a clerk, a translator, or a revenue collector, you had to write Urdu in the Persian script. Hindu elites flocked to the schools to master it.

Consider the case of Dhanpat Rai Srivastava, born in a small village near Varanasi. We know him today by his pen name, Munshi Premchand, the father of modern Urdu and Hindi fiction. He began his career writing exclusively in Urdu, publishing stories in the journal *Zamana*, edited by Munshi Daya Narain Nigam, another Hindu Kayastha. For Premchand, writing in Urdu was natural. It was the medium through which he could reach the educated public of northern India.

How did this shared world fracture? The division was not organic. In the late nineteenth century, British colonial policies began classifying citizens by religion, pushing a neat divide between Hindi for Hindus and Urdu for Muslims. Language was weaponized as a tool of political mobilization. The introduction of print technology forced a choice between scripts: the Devanagari script became linked to Hindu nationalism, while the Nastaliq script was claimed by Muslim political movements.

What started as a tool of administration and elite culture was slowly carved in two. Yet, the texts, the administrative records, and the letters of the Kayasthas, Pandits, and Khatris remain. They stand as quiet proof that Urdu was never the language of a single faith, but the creation of a shared class that ran an empire.
