# Polishing the Street Tongue

Did you know that the creation of Urdu as a high literary language was a deliberate act of engineering? Khan-e-Arzu and his contemporaries did not just write in the vernacular; they systematically polished it. They stripped out local words they deemed too rustic and replaced them with Persian syntax and vocabulary. 

This created a language that was local enough to be understood, yet Persianized enough to maintain their class distance from the common people. As the provinces of Awadh and Hyderabad broke away from the dying center in Delhi, their rulers eagerly adopted this new Urdu. The local language became the badge of a new domestic aristocracy. It was a way to claim authority without relying on the ghost of a Persian empire that could no longer protect them.

By the late eighteenth century, the transformation was complete. The great poet Mir Taqi Mir wrote his heartbreaking ghazals not in the elite Persian of his ancestors, but in the language of the Delhi bazaars. The old Persian scholars had sneered at the local dialect.

Now, Mir proudly declared that his poetry was written in the language of the steps of the Jama Masjid. The loss of wealth had forced the Mughals to look inward. Out of the ashes of their economic empire, they built a literary monument that outlasted their rule.
