[[T.O.C (Philosophy)|Up to Philosophy]]

> **Seed:** "what does it mean to live authentically according to the existentialists"

We often speak of "finding ourselves" as if we were a set of lost keys waiting in the back of a drawer. We imagine a core, a soul, a static essence that, once uncovered, will provide the map for the rest of our lives. But what if there is nothing to find? What if the drawer is empty? This is the starting point for the existentialist: the terrifying, exhilarating realization that existence precedes essence. You are not a seed destined to become an oak; you are a burst of consciousness with no blueprint at all. To live authentically is not to discover who you are, but to acknowledge that you are the one doing the inventing.

Consider the waiter in a Parisian cafe. He moves with a certain precision, a choreographed stiffness. He is a little too quick to take the order, a little too attentive, a little too much like a "waiter." Sartre saw this as a performance. The man is trying to be a waiter in the same way an inkwell is an inkwell. He wants to coincide with his job, to escape the burden of his own freedom by pretending he has a fixed nature. He is acting in bad faith. He has traded his messy, unpredictable humanity for the safety of a role. Most of us do this every day. We act as "the father," "the student," "the professional," hoping the script will tell us what to do next so we don't have to decide for ourselves.

But we cannot blame the waiter entirely. We are born into a world we did not choose, with bodies we did not design and histories we did not write. This is what the existentialists call facticity. You are born in a specific city, to specific parents, with a specific set of talents or limitations. To ignore these facts is not authenticity; it is delusion. You cannot simply "identify" as a bird and expect to fly. Authenticity, then, is the tension between these two poles: your facticity (the things you cannot change) and your transcendence (the fact that you are always more than your circumstances). It is the act of taking the raw materials of your life and consciously shaping them into a project.

The greatest enemy of this project is the crowd. Heidegger called it "the They" (Das Man). The "They" is that anonymous voice that tells us what is fashionable, what is moral, and what constitutes a successful life. When we follow the "They," we are never the ones living. We eat what "one" eats, we think what "one" thinks, and we die as "one" dies. It is comfortable. It removes the sting of individual responsibility. If everyone is doing it, how can it be wrong? But this comfort is a sedative. It dulls the edge of life until we are merely ghosts inhabiting our own bodies.

True authenticity requires a break from this anesthesia. It usually arrives as anxiety. We often treat anxiety as a symptom to be cured, a chemical imbalance to be corrected. For the existentialist, anxiety is the opposite: it is the hallmark of health. It is the vertigo we feel when we realize that nothing—not God, not society, not biology—is holding us up. We are standing on a tightrope over an abyss, and we are the ones holding the pole. To live authentically is to refuse to look away from that abyss. It is to make a choice knowing there is no guarantee it is the "right" one, and then to own that choice with every fiber of your being.

Does this mean authenticity is a destination? Can we finally "arrive" and be authentic forever? No. The moment you think you have achieved authenticity, you have turned it into an essence, a trophy to put on a shelf. You have become the waiter again. Authenticity is a flickering flame, something that must be re-asserted in every moment of decision. It is the constant, exhausting effort to remain awake in a world that desperately wants you to sleep. It is not about being "true to yourself," because there is no "self" to be true to. It is about being true to the freedom that allows you to create a self in the first place.
