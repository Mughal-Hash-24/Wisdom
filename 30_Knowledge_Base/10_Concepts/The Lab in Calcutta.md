# The Lab in Calcutta

Calcutta, 1800. John Borthwick Gilchrist, a Scottish surgeon who had abandoned medicine to study oriental tongues, stood in the newly founded Fort William College. Before him lay a chaotic linguistic landscape. The street spoke Hindustani. It was a fluid, shared vernacular born of markets, military camps, and Sufi shrines, defying easy classification. To the British East India Company, this fluid tongue was an administrative nightmare. How do you govern a population whose very speech refuses to sit still inside a grammar book?

## The Fluid Vernacular

Before Governor-General Richard Wellesley founded the college, northern India breathed a linguistic continuum. High administration ran on Persian, the elegant legacy of the Mughal Empire. The common folk spoke regional dialects of Khariboli, Braj Bhasha, and Awadhi. In these spoken forms, Arabic, Persian, Sanskrit, and local terms mingled freely. A scribe could write the same spoken sentence in Persian script or Devanagari, depending on his training and audience. It was a language of pragmatism, not of purity.

Why do you think the British found this fluidity so threatening?

To an empire built on ledger books and racial categorization, a shared, hybrid language was a security risk. If Hindus and Muslims spoke and wrote the same vernacular, the boundaries between communities remained fuzzy. Wellesley faced a crisis of control. The Company had shifted from merchants to rulers, yet their young writers—teenagers sent from Britain to collect taxes and run courts—could not talk to the local population. They were alien rulers in a vast land. Fort William College was designed to turn these raw boys into disciplined civil servants.

## The Laboratory of Partition

Gilchrist, appointed professor of Hindustani, faced a massive constraint. He had to teach a language that had no standardized textbooks. He believed that to make Hindustani teachable to British minds, it had to be made systematic. To do this, he decided to split it. He saw the existing hybrid vernacular as a corruption, an unstable mixture that required purification.

Here is what most people miss about this: the division of Hindi and Urdu was not an ancient religious reality, but a colonial project designed for classroom convenience.

Gilchrist hired local scholars, pundits and munshis, and set them to work. He instructed them to strip Hindustani of its natural hybridity. For the Hindu pundits, the task was to write prose by purging Persian and Arabic loanwords, replacing them with Sanskrit vocabulary, and writing in the Devanagari script. This created "Hindi" as a high-register prose language. For the Muslim munshis, the task was to elevate the Persianized register, writing in the Nastaliq script and borrowing heavily from Persian and Arabic. This created "Urdu."

These were not natural developments. They were laboratory creations. For the first time, these registers were codified as separate, mutually exclusive standards, published in textbooks printed on the college’s own presses.

## Administrative Incentives

The British administrative incentives for this division were simple and self-serving. By creating two distinct registers, the British could categorize their subjects. They could recruit separate pools of civil servants—Hindus who mastered the Sanskritized version and Muslims who mastered the Persianized version.

This split replaced a complex, shared administrative class. Historically, bilingual scribes had moved effortlessly between scripts and registers. Now, the colonial state demanded a choice. By dividing the administration into Urdu-writing and Hindi-writing factions, the British created a system where each group looked to the colonial state as the patron of their linguistic rights. The division made the native bureaucracy easier to manage and less likely to unite against their rulers.

## The Textbook Economy

The printing press at Fort William turned these artificial registers into economic commodities. Before print, manuscripts were copied by hand, a slow process that allowed local variations. Textbooks printed in thousands of copies locked the grammar and vocabulary in place.

Did you know that the college paid writers handsomely to produce these books? A single successful translation could secure a writer's financial future. This created a new economy of publishing, where state patronage decided which language was profitable to learn. Hindu and Muslim schools were forced to adopt these standardized texts to prepare their students for government service. The market followed the state: if you wanted a government job, you had to buy the college-approved grammar book.

## Second- and Third-Order Effects

Nobody in 1800 predicted that a pedagogical experiment in Calcutta would lay the groundwork for the partition of a subcontinent.

The immediate consequence was the erosion of the shared middle ground. As print capitalism grew, regional dialects and hybrid registers were pushed to the margins. To be educated meant to choose a side.

The second-order effect was the politicization of the script itself. By the late nineteenth century, the Hindi-Urdu controversy became a proxy for political power, jobs, and religious identity. The script became a flag. To write in Devanagari was to declare oneself a Hindu nationalist; to write in Nastaliq was to align with Muslim political interests.

The third-order effect was the geographical partition of 1947. The linguistic division, institutionalized in the textbooks of Fort William College, became the cultural engine of the two-nation theory. The lines drawn in Gilchrist’s classrooms eventually became international borders. Grammar books can be as lethal as bayonets.
