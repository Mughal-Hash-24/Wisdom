# The Invasion of the Vernacular

Here’s what most people miss about this linguistic transition: it was not a sudden, romantic embrace of the mother tongue. It was a calculated rescue mission for elite status. Around 1700, a poet named Vali Dakhani arrived in Delhi from the south, carrying a manuscript of poems written in Rekhta, the "mixed" vernacular. 

Until then, Delhi's intellectuals dismissed Rekhta as a crude street dialect, unfit for high art. Vali’s verses changed everything. He proved that this local language, rich with Sanskrit-derived verbs and Arabic-Persian nouns, could express the same complex Sufi longings and courtly complaints as classical Persian. The young aristocrats of Delhi, facing economic ruin, seized upon Vali’s style. They realized they did not need expensive imports to be cultured.
