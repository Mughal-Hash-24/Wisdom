# Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din (The Revival of Religious Sciences)

[[T.O.C (Psychology_Philosophy)|Up to Psychology & Philosophy]]

## Summary
**The Revival of Religious Sciences** (*Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din*) is the magnum opus of the 11th-century polymath **Abu Hamid al-Ghazali**. It is widely considered one of the most influential works in Islamic history. The book aims to bridge the gap between legalistic ritualism (*Fiqh*) and spiritual intuition (*Sufism*). Al-Ghazali wrote it to "revive" the spiritual heart of religious practice, arguing that outward actions are meaningless without inner sincerity and ethical refinement.

## Key Themes
1.  **Integration of Outer and Inner:** Rituals like prayer and fasting are not just legal obligations but spiritual exercises designed to purify the heart.
2.  **The Heart (Qalb):** The spiritual heart is the seat of knowledge and character. Its health determines a person's ultimate fate.
3.  **Knowledge ('Ilm):** True knowledge is that which leads to action and spiritual transformation, not just intellectual debate.
4.  **Destructive vs. Saving Attributes:** The human soul is a battleground between vices (pride, envy) and virtues (patience, gratitude).

## Significance
Al-Ghazali successfully legitimized Sufism (tasawwuf) within mainstream Islamic orthodoxy. By demonstrating that spiritual purification is a necessary component of the Sharia, he created a synthesis that defined Sunni Islam for centuries.
