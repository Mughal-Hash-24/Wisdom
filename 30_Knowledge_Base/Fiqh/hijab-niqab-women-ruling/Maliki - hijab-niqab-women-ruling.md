# Maliki Position: What are the rulings on hijab and nikab for women with exact rulings on the fard, the sunnah etc?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - hijab-niqab-women-ruling|Back to Synthesis]]

**School:** Maliki
**Methodological Disposition:** Empiricist. Unique use of Medinan practice (amal ahl al-Madina) and maslaha mursala as independent legal sources.
**Query Type:** CLASSICAL POSITION

---

## Ruling

In the dominant (mu'tamad) Maliki position, the **Hijab**—defined as covering the entire body except for the face and the hands (up to the wrists)—is **Fard** (Obligatory) for a free woman in the presence of non-mahram men. 

The **Niqab** (face-veil) is generally classified as **Mandub** (Recommended) or permissible. It is not considered Fard because the face is not part of the *awra* (nakedness) of a woman. However, if a woman is exceptionally beautiful and there is a verified fear of *fitna* (social discord or temptation), covering the face may be regarded as **Wajib** (Obligatory) as a preventative measure (*Sadd al-Dhara'i*), though the face itself remains technically non-awra.

**Classical Reference:** *Al-Mudawwana* by Sahnun (recording the views of Imam Malik) and *Bidayat al-Mujtahid* by Ibn Rushd al-Hafid (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Quran, Amal ahl al-Madina, and Qiyas.

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Surah An-Nur (Q 24:31) — "...and not expose their adornment except that which [necessarily] appears thereof..."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** Revealed to establish social decorum and modesty among the early Muslim community in Medina, distinguishing free women from others and preventing harassment.
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* Muhkam (Clear) regarding the obligation to cover, but the phrase "except that which appears thereof" is open to interpretation.
  - *Amm or Khass:* Amm (General) for all believing free women.
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation applies to the core obligation of modesty.
- **Juristic Reading:** The Maliki school, following the interpretation of Ibn Abbas and the practice of the people of Medina, identifies "what appears thereof" as the face and hands. Therefore, the command to cover does not extend to these two areas.

*If Amal ahl al-Madina (Medinan Practice):*
- **The Practice:** The women of Medina during the era of the Sahaba and Tabi'un were known to participate in public life, markets, and the mosque with their faces uncovered. 
- **Why This Source:** Imam Malik viewed the continuous, inherited practice of the people of Medina as a "living Sunnah" that provides the definitive context for Quranic commands. Since the Medinan community—the direct inheritors of the Prophet’s (pbuh) guidance—did not treat the face as awra, the Maliki school treats this as a decisive proof over solitary (ahad) hadiths that might suggest otherwise.
- **Scope:** This is treated as a universal ruling for the school, grounding the definition of *awra* in observed communal tradition.

*If Qiyas:*
- **Original Case (Asl):** Prayer (Salah) and the state of Ritual Consecration (Ihram).
- **New Case (Far'):** Presence in public/non-mahram settings.
- **Effective Cause (Illah):** The definition of what constitutes the essential human persona and what is necessary for functional interaction.
- **Extended Ruling (Hukm):** Since a woman is required to uncover her face during Hajj (Ihram) and is permitted to pray with her face uncovered, the face cannot be inherently *awra*. If it were *awra*, covering it would be mandatory in prayer.
- **Why This Illah:** The Maliki school prioritizes the consistency of the definition of *awra* across different spheres of law.

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
The Hanbali school differs significantly, often interpreting the same Quranic verse to mean that the face is part of the "adornment" that must be hidden, thus making the Niqab Fard. The Maliki school rejects this by prioritizing *Amal ahl al-Madina* and the functional necessity of the face for identification and testimony in legal matters (Maslaha).

---

## Internal Dissent

A significant minority view exists among later Maliki jurists (particularly in the Maghreb and Andalusia), such as Al-Qurtubi. They argued that in times of widespread corruption (*fasad al-zaman*), the Niqab becomes **Wajib** (Obligatory) not because the face is awra, but due to the principle of **Sadd al-Dhara'i** (Blocking the Means to Harm). The usul reason for divergence here is the weighting of *Maslaha* (Public Interest) over the original *Asl* (the default ruling of permissibility).

---

## Notes

In North and West African Maliki tradition, the "Litham" (a form of face covering) was often a cultural or status-based garment rather than a purely religious requirement, reflecting the school's ability to harmonize local custom (*Urf*) with the foundational legal requirement of the Hijab.
