# Hanbali Position: What are the rulings on hijab and nikab for women with exact rulings on the fard, the sunnah etc?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - hijab-niqab-women-ruling|Back to Synthesis]]

**School:** Hanbali
**Methodological Disposition:** Textualist. Strongest reliance on hadith corpus, including weak narrations; most resistant to independent qiyas and maslaha.
**Query Type:** [CLASSICAL POSITION]

---

## Ruling

The dominant (*mu'tamad*) position of the Hanbali school is that the entire body of a free woman (*al-hurrah*) is considered *awrah* (nakedness) in the presence of non-mahram men, including the face and the hands. Consequently, the **Niqab (face covering) is Obligatory (Wajib/Fard)**, and the **Hijab (head and body covering) is Obligatory (Wajib/Fard)**. There is no distinction in the school's authoritative later works between the face and the rest of the body regarding the requirement to cover.

**Classical Reference:** *Al-Insaf fi Ma'rifat al-Rajih min al-Khilaf* by Al-Mardawi (VERIFIED); *Al-Mughni* by Ibn Qudama (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Quran and Sunnah.

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Surah Al-Ahzab (Q 33:59) — "O Prophet, tell your wives and your daughters and the women of the believers to bring down over themselves [part] of their outer garments (jalabib)..."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** No specific occasion of revelation recorded for this specific verse, though it is part of the broader legislative context of the Hijab verses in Medina aimed at distinguishing free believing women from others and protecting them from harassment.
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* Muhkam (Clear in its command to wear the Jilbab).
  - *Amm or Khass:* Amm (General to all believing women).
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation applies here; this verse is considered the final legislation on public dress.
- **Juristic Reading:** The Hanbali school interprets the "bringing down of the Jilbab" (idna') as covering the entire body from the head down, including the face. They rely on the explanation of Ibn Abbas and the practice of the early generations that this garment should cover the forehead and be drawn over the face, leaving only one eye (or both) to see.

*If Sunnah:*
- **Hadith:** "The woman is awrah (nakedness); when she goes out, the Shaytan seeks to lead her astray." — narrated by Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, reported in Tirmidhi (VERIFIED).
- **Authentication Status:** Sahih. The Hanbali school treats this as a foundational text.
- **Asbab al-Wurud:** A general statement by the Prophet (peace be upon him) regarding the nature of the woman's presence in public.
- **Scope:** Treated as *Amm* (General).
- **Application:** Since the Prophet (peace be upon him) defined "the woman" (the whole person) as *awrah*, the Hanbalis argue that every part is included in the obligation to cover unless a specific, authentic text exempts a part. They find the evidence for exempting the face and hands to be either weak or overridden by the general command.

*If Ijma:*
- **Consensus Body:** Not claimed as universal across all four schools, but often cited as the *mashhur* (well-known) practice and requirement within the Hanbali tradition for the protection of society.
- **Period:** Classical and late-classical eras.
- **Scope:** Specific to the Hanbali tradition's dominant legal preference.

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
The Hanbali school differs from the Hanafi and Maliki schools primarily on the interpretation of Surah An-Nur (24:31): "except what appears thereof." While Hanafis and Malikis argue this refers to the face and hands (based on certain reports from the Sahaba), the Hanbali school argues it refers to the outer garment itself (the Jilbab) or what is accidentally uncovered by the wind. Methodologically, the Hanbali school prioritizes the general prohibition ("The woman is awrah") over the specific exemption ("except what appears thereof") because they view the evidence for the face/hands exemption as non-definitive.

---

## Internal Dissent

There is an older report from Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal and some early scholars of the school (and also found in some passages of *Al-Mughni*) suggesting that the face and hands are not *awrah* in the context of prayer (*salat*), but they are *awrah* in front of non-mahram men. 

Furthermore, **Ibn Taymiyya** and **Ibn al-Qayyim** (major Hanbali figures) provide a nuanced view: they distinguish between the *awrah* in prayer and the *awrah* of "looking." They concluded that even if the face is not *awrah* in prayer, it is still mandatory to cover it in public to prevent *fitna* (temptation). This solidified the later Hanbali consensus that Niqab is mandatory.

---

## Notes

The Hanbali position is the most restrictive on this issue and forms the basis of the legal practice in modern-day Saudi Arabia and Qatar. In the Hanbali tradition, the Sunnah aspect of dress refers to the *quality* and *modesty* of the garment (not being see-through, not being an adornment in itself), but the *act* of covering the face remains within the realm of *wajib* (obligatory) rather than merely *mustahabb* (recommended).
