# Maliki Position: What are the most important habits a Muslim should build?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - essential-muslim-habits|View Synthesis]]

**School:** Maliki
**Methodological Disposition:** Empiricist. Unique use of Medinan practice (amal ahl al-Madina) and maslaha mursala as independent legal sources.
**Query Type:** CLASSICAL POSITION

---

## Ruling

The most important habits for a Muslim are hierarchically structured based on their legal status. The primary habit is the preservation of the **Fara'id** (Obligations), specifically the Five Pillars, with an emphasis on the communal performance of Prayer. Following these are the **Sunan Mu'akkada** (Emphasized Prophetic Practices), such as the Witr prayer and the Eid prayers. Finally, **Mandub** (Recommended) actions and **Adab** (Etiquettes) form the protective outer layer of a Muslim’s character, heavily influenced by the "living sunnah" as preserved in Medinan practice.

**Classical Reference:** *Al-Mudawwana al-Kubra*, Sahnun (VERIFIED); *Al-Muwatta*, Malik ibn Anas (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Quran, Sunnah, and Amal ahl al-Madina.

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Al-Baqarah (Q 2:43) — "And establish prayer and give zakah and bow with those who bow."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** Revealed in Medina to establish the structural communal duties of the new Muslim polity.
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* Muhkam (Clear) regarding the obligation of Prayer and Zakat.
  - *Amm or Khass:* Amm (General) for all believers.
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation; these remain the permanent pillars of the faith.
- **Juristic Reading:** The Maliki school emphasizes "bow with those who bow" as a textual basis for the habit of *congregational* prayer being a central, almost defining, communal habit.

*If Sunnah:*
- **Hadith:** "Islam is built upon five [pillars]..." — narrated by Ibn Umar, reported in Al-Muwatta and the Sahihayn (VERIFIED).
- **Authentication Status:** Sahih.
- **Asbab al-Wurud:** The Prophet (s) stated this to define the essential boundaries of the faith to both new converts and established companions.
- **Scope:** General (Aam) for all Muslims.
- **Application:** This establishes the "hierarchy of habits." The five pillars are not merely acts but *habits of being* (e.g., the habit of constant prayerful orientation, the annual habit of fasting).

*If Amal ahl al-Madina (Medinan Practice):*
- **The Practice:** The collective adherence to the prayer times, the communal nature of the mosque, and the specific form of the Adhan in Medina.
- **Why This Source:** Imam Malik viewed the continuous, inherited practice of the people of Medina as a "transmitted sunnah" (Sunnah Manqula) that is more reliable than a single-chain hadith (khabar al-wahid). If the people of Medina collectively maintained a habit (like the specific way of standing in prayer), it is considered the most authentic Prophetic habit.
- **Scope:** It serves as a definitive filter for how the Sunnah is applied in daily life.

*If Maslaha Mursala (Public Interest):*
- **The Interest:** The habit of seeking "Beneficial Knowledge" (Al-Ilm al-Nafi').
- **Why Unattested:** While general texts encourage knowledge, the specific *habit* of systematic study is a matter of public interest to ensure the preservation of the Sharia.
- **Limits:** Must not contradict an established text and must serve the "Five Essentials" (religion, life, intellect, lineage, property).

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
The Maliki school differs from the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools by prioritizing *Amal ahl al-Madina* over isolated Hadith. For instance, in the habit of fasting the six days of Shawwal, Imam Malik famously disliked making it a continuous, public habit immediately after Eid, fearing the common people would eventually view it as an obligatory extension of Ramadan (a *Maslaha* concern).

---

## Internal Dissent

There is internal debate regarding the status of the **Witr** prayer. While some schools (Hanafi) view it as Wajib, the dominant Maliki view is that it is a *Sunna Mu'akkada* (Highly Emphasized Sunnah). However, some earlier Medinan authorities treated it with such gravity that omitting it habitually was seen as a sign of deficient character (*'adala*).

---

## Notes

In North and West African Maliki traditions, the habit of reciting a "Hizb" of Quran collectively in the mosque (the *Lahun* or *Qira'at al-Jama'ah*) became a deeply entrenched communal habit based on *Maslaha*—ensuring the community remains connected to the Quran even if individual literacy is low.
