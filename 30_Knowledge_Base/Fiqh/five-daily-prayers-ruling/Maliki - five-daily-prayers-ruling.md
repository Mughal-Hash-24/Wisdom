# Maliki Position: What is the ruling on the five daily prayers?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - five-daily-prayers-ruling|Back to Synthesis]]

**School:** Maliki
**Methodological Disposition:** Empiricist. Unique use of Medinan practice (amal ahl al-Madina) and maslaha mursala as independent legal sources.
**Query Type:** CLASSICAL POSITION

---

## Ruling

The five daily prayers (Salat) are an individual obligation (**Fard 'Ayn**) upon every sane, adult Muslim who has reached the age of puberty. Neglecting them without a valid legal excuse is considered a major sin. In the Maliki tradition, the obligation is so central that the school discusses the legal consequences of persistent abandonment as a matter of public order and religious integrity.

**Classical Reference:** *Al-Muwatta*, Imam Malik ibn Anas (VERIFIED); *Al-Mudawwana al-Kubra*, Sahnun ibn Sa'id (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Amal ahl al-Madina.

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Al-Baqarah (Q 2:43) — "And establish prayer and give zakah and bow with those who bow [in worship and obedience]."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** No specific occasion of revelation recorded for this specific verse in terms of initiating the five prayers (which were established during the Isra' and Mi'raj), but it serves as the foundational legislative command for the Medinan community.
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* **Muhkam** (Clear in meaning).
  - *Amm or Khass:* **Amm** (General) to all believers.
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation applies; the obligation is perpetual.
- **Juristic Reading:** The Maliki school treats the imperative "Aqimu" (establish) as a definitive command (amr jazim) creating a binding obligation (fard).

*If Sunnah:*
- **Hadith:** "Islam is built upon five..." — narrated by Ibn Umar, reported in Al-Bukhari and Muslim (VERIFIED)
- **Authentication Status:** Sahih.
- **Asbab al-Wurud:** The Prophet (ﷺ) was defining the pillars of the faith to summarize the essence of Islam for his followers.
- **Scope:** Universal (Aam).
- **Application:** The inclusion of Salat as the second pillar of Islam establishes its character as a prerequisite for the faith's practice and a foundational obligation.

*If Ijma:*
- **Consensus Body:** The Companions, Tabi'un, and all subsequent generations of Muslim jurists.
- **Period:** From the Prophetic era to the present.
- **Scope:** Universal across all schools.
- **Note:** This is an absolute consensus (*Ijma' Qat'i*) regarding the number and obligation of the prayers.

*If Amal ahl al-Madina (Medinan Practice):*
- **The Practice:** The continuous, communal performance of the five daily prayers in the city of Medina, led by the Prophet (ﷺ) and then the Rightly Guided Caliphs, transmitted from generation to generation.
- **Why This Source:** For Imam Malik, the *Amal* (practice) of the people of Medina is a definitive source of law. He argued that it is impossible for the entire population of the Prophet's city to be mistaken about a daily, public ritual performed five times a day. This "living Sunnah" is considered superior to solitary reports (*khabar al-wahid*) in the Maliki usul.
- **Scope:** The practice confirms both the obligation and the specific form (timings and number) of the prayers.

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
The Maliki school generally treats *Fard* and *Wajib* as synonymous for the five prayers, unlike the Hanafi school which creates a hierarchical distinction based on the strength of the evidence (*qat'i* vs *dhanni*). Furthermore, the Maliki school relies heavily on the *Amal* of Medina to determine the specifics of prayer (such as *sadl* or praying with arms at the sides), sometimes favoring this communal transmission over individual Hadiths that might suggest different postures.

---

## Internal Dissent

No significant internal dissent exists on the fundamental obligation of the five daily prayers. Discussions within the school primarily focus on the legal status of one who abandons them (whether they are a *kafir* or a *fasiq*) and the status of certain supererogatory prayers like *Witr*, which Malikis categorize as *Sunnah Mu'akkadah* rather than *Wajib*.

---

## Notes

The Maliki school is historically noted for its emphasis on the public nature of prayer. The *Muwatta* of Imam Malik begins its section on prayer not just with the obligation, but with the timings (*wuqut*), reflecting the school's concern with the empirical and temporal reality of the ritual as practiced in the Prophet's city.
