# Hanafi Position: What is the ruling on the five daily prayers?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - five-daily-prayers-ruling|Back to Synthesis]]

**School:** Hanafi
**Methodological Disposition:** Rationalist. Heaviest use of qiyas and istihsan of all four schools.
**Query Type:** CLASSICAL POSITION

---

## Ruling

The five daily prayers (Salat) are **Fard 'Ayn** (individually obligatory) upon every mukallaf (sane, pubescent Muslim). Denying their status as an obligation constitutes *kufr* (disbelief) because the requirement is established by *dalil qat'i* (definitive evidence). Neglecting them out of laziness without denying their obligation is a major sin (*fasiq*) but does not remove one from the fold of Islam, though the individual must be disciplined and compelled to perform them.

**Classical Reference:** *Al-Hidaya* by Al-Marghinani (VERIFIED); *Bada'i al-Sana'i* by Al-Kasani (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma.

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Surah An-Nisa (Q 4:103) — "...Indeed, prayer has been decreed upon the believers a decree of specified times."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** Revealed in the context of the Prayer of Fear (*Salat al-Khawf*) during military campaigns, emphasizing that even in danger, the obligation remains.
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* **Muhkam** (Clear and definitive). The word "Kitaban" implies a written, fixed obligation.
  - *Amm or Khass:* **Amm** (General). It applies to all believers across all times and places.
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation applies to the fundamental obligation of the five prayers, though the specific number (reduced from fifty to five) is established via Sunnah (Isra and Mi'raj).
- **Juristic Reading:** The Hanafi school treats the command in the imperative form as establishing *Fardiyat* (strict obligation) when coupled with definitive evidence of its permanence.

*If Sunnah:*
- **Hadith:** "Islam is built upon five: testifying that there is no god but Allah... and establishing the prayer..." — narrated by Ibn Umar, reported in Bukhari and Muslim (VERIFIED).
- **Authentication Status:** **Mutawatir** (Massively transmitted) in meaning.
- **Asbab al-Wurud:** General instruction to the Sahaba defining the pillars of the faith.
- **Scope:** General (*Amm*).
- **Application:** The Prophet (peace be upon him) consistently performed and taught these five prayers, and the Hanafi school views this continuous practice (*Sunnah Mutawatira*) as a definitive proof for the *Fard* status.

*If Ijma:*
- **Consensus Body:** All Companions and subsequent generations.
- **Period:** Established since the Meccan period (post-Mi'raj) and consolidated in Medina.
- **Scope:** Universal across all schools and sects of Islam.
- **Note:** This is an *Ijma Qat'i* (definitive consensus); there is no recorded disagreement among any Muslim jurist in history regarding the obligation of the five prayers.

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
While the five prayers are *Fard*, the Hanafi school diverges from the other three schools on the status of the **Witr** prayer. Hanafis classify Witr as **Wajib** (necessary) rather than Sunnah, based on the principle that evidence from *Ahad* (solitary) narrations can establish necessity (*Wujub*) but not definitive obligation (*Fardiyat*). This distinction is unique to Hanafi usul.

---

## Internal Dissent

There is no dissent regarding the **obligation** of the five daily prayers. However, there is internal discussion regarding the definition of the start and end times for specific prayers (notably 'Asr). Imam Abu Hanifa's original position (the *mithlayn* or double-shadow rule) for the start of 'Asr differs from his disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), who followed the single-shadow rule. The later *mu'tamad* (relied upon) position in the school often allows for the disciples' view.

---

## Notes

In the Hanafi tradition, the legal consequences for missing prayer differ from the Hanbali school. In Hanafi fiqh, the person is jailed or disciplined until they pray, but they are not executed as an apostate unless they explicitly deny the obligation itself. This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on the distinction between *amal* (action) and *iman* (faith).
