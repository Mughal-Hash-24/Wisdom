# Hanafi Position: What is the ruling on musical instruments?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - musical-instruments-ruling|Back to Synthesis]]

**School:** Hanafi
**Methodological Disposition:** Rationalist. Heaviest use of qiyas and istihsan of all four schools.
**Query Type:** CLASSICAL POSITION

---

## Ruling

The dominant (mu'tamad) Hanafi position is that the use and listening to musical instruments is **impermissible (Makruh Tahrimi/Haraam)**. This applies to stringed, wind, and percussion instruments that are designed for entertainment (*lahw*). An exception is made for the *duff* (tambourine without rattles) specifically for weddings and celebrations, provided it does not lead to prohibited behavior.

**Classical Reference:** *Al-Hidaya* by Al-Marghinani (VERIFIED); *Radd al-Muhtar* by Ibn Abidin (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Quran, Sunnah, and Qiyas.

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Surah Luqman (Q 31:6) — "And of the people is he who purchases the idle talk (lahw al-hadith) to mislead [others] from the way of Allah..."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** While some reports suggest it referred to Nadr ibn al-Harith purchasing stories of Persian kings to distract people from the Quran, the Hanafi mufassirun extend the application.
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* Mutashabih (metaphorical/open to interpretation regarding what constitutes "idle talk").
  - *Amm or Khass:* Amm (General). The term *lahw* encompasses anything that distracts from the remembrance of Allah.
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation applies here.
- **Juristic Reading:** The Hanafi school, following the interpretation of Ibn Mas'ud (who resided in Kufa, the cradle of the school), identifies *lahw al-hadith* as including singing and musical instruments.

*If Sunnah:*
- **Hadith:** "There will be people from my Ummah who will seek to make lawful: silk, alcohol, and musical instruments (ma'azif)." — narrated by Abu Malik al-Ash'ari, reported in Sahih al-Bukhari (VERIFIED).
- **Authentication Status:** Sahih.
- **Asbab al-Wurud:** The Prophet (PBUH) was describing future signs of moral decay where prohibited things would be treated as permissible.
- **Scope:** The Hanafi school treats the term *ma'azif* as a general category for all stringed and woodwind instruments.
- **Application:** The pairing of musical instruments with alcohol and silk (for men) indicates a prohibition of a similar degree, categorizing them as tools of *lahw* (frivolous diversion).

*If Qiyas:*
- **Original Case (Asl):** Consumption of intoxicants (Khamr).
- **New Case (Far'):** Professional or recreational music.
- **Effective Cause (Illah):** *Sakar* (intoxication of the senses) and *Tahrik al-Nafs* (arousal of the lower self/passions) leading to the neglect of religious obligations (*dhikr*).
- **Extended Ruling (Hukm):** Prohibited/Makruh Tahrimi.
- **Why This Illah:** The Hanafi school accepts this *illah* because the rational objective of Sharia is to preserve the intellect and the heart's focus on the Creator. Instruments that are "designed for play" (*alat al-malahi*) are seen as intrinsically linked to environments of sin (assemblies of drinking and immorality).

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
The Hanafi school is notably stricter than the Maliki school on this point. While Malikis might allow certain folk music or instruments not associated with "sinful gatherings," the Hanafi school uses *Sadd al-Dhara'i* (blocking the means) and a broad *Qiyas* on *lahw* to prohibit the instruments themselves, regardless of the content of the music, because the instruments are fashioned specifically for a purpose that distracts from Allah.

---

## Internal Dissent

There is a significant nuance regarding the **Duff**. Abu Yusuf (the primary student of Abu Hanifa) permitted the use of the *duff* in weddings and festivities, as it was established in the Sunnah. However, he maintained the prohibition on instruments with bells or those used in professional musical entertainment. Some later Hanafi scholars in the Ottoman era also distinguished between "military music" (Mehter) used for psychological strength in Jihad and "entertainment music," showing an application of *Istihsan* (juristic preference) for state utility.

---

## Notes

In the South Asian Hanafi tradition (Deobandi/Barelvi), this prohibition is strictly maintained. However, in the context of Sufi practice (Sama'), some Hanafis historically allowed certain forms of vocal music or specific instruments, though this was often criticized by the mainstream *fuqaha* (jurists) of the school as a deviation from the *mu'tamad* (relied-upon) position.
