# Shafi'i Position: What is the ruling on musical instruments?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - musical-instruments-ruling|Back to Synthesis]]

**School:** Shafi'i
**Methodological Disposition:** Systematic textualist. Al-Shafi'i formalized usul al-fiqh itself; strict qiyas within bounded methodology; skeptical of maslaha mursala and istihsan.
**Query Type:** [CLASSICAL POSITION]

---

## Ruling

The dominant (*mu'tamad*) Shafi'i position is that the use of most musical instruments is prohibited (*haram*). Specifically, stringed instruments (*ma'azif*) and wind instruments (*mazamir* / *nay*) are strictly forbidden, whether played alone or in accompaniment. The only consistent exception is the *duff* (a frame drum/tambourine without bells or jingles), which is permissible (*mubah*) and even recommended (*mandub*) for weddings and Eids.

**Classical Reference:** *Minhaj al-Talibin* by Imam al-Nawawi (VERIFIED); *Al-Umm* by Imam al-Shafi'i (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Sunnah (supported by Quranic exegesis and Qiyas)

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Luqman (Q 31:6) — "And of mankind is he who purchases idle talks (*lahw al-hadith*) to mislead from the path of Allah without knowledge..."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** Reported that Nadr ibn al-Harith would purchase singing girls to distract people from hearing the Quran recited by the Prophet (saw).
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* While the verse is *muhkam* in its prohibition of misleading from Allah's path, the term "idle talk" (*lahw al-hadith*) is subject to interpretation.
  - *Amm or Khass:* General (*amm*) in its wording but often applied to specific distractions like music and singing.
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation applies here.
- **Juristic Reading:** Shafi'i jurists, following the interpretation of Sahaba like Ibn Mas'ud and Ibn Abbas, include musical instruments under "idle talk" when they serve no religious or necessary purpose and distract from the dhikr of Allah.

*If Sunnah:*
- **Hadith:** "There will be from my Ummah people who will seek to make lawful: fornication, silk, intoxicants, and musical instruments (*al-ma'azif*)." — narrated by Abu Malik al-Ash'ari, reported in Sahih al-Bukhari (VERIFIED)
- **Authentication Status:** Sahih. (Note: Some later critics like Ibn Hazm questioned the connection of the chain, but the Shafi'i school accepts the hadith as authentic and binding).
- **Asbab al-Wurud:** A prophetic warning regarding future generations who would normalize major sins by grouping them with permissible-looking activities.
- **Scope:** General (*aam*) prohibition of instruments (*ma'azif*).
- **Application:** The grouping of musical instruments with major sins like *zina* (fornication) and *khamr* (intoxication) provides a strong legal indication (*dalalah*) of their categorical prohibition.

*If Qiyas:*
- **Original Case (Asl):** Intoxicants (*Khamr*).
- **New Case (Far'):** Stringed and wind instruments that produce *tarab* (musical rapture/ecstasy).
- **Effective Cause (Illah):** Inducing a state of "unmindfulness" or emotional manipulation that distracts the heart from the Creator and leads to the neglect of obligations.
- **Extended Ruling (Hukm):** Prohibition (*Tahrim*).
- **Why This Illah:** Al-Shafi'i emphasized that the law aims to protect the intellect and the heart; instruments that specifically target the emotions in a way that mimics or leads to intoxication are forbidden based on the textual evidence linking them to sinful gatherings.

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
The Shafi'i school rejects the use of *Istihsan* (juristic preference) which might allow music based on perceived beauty or emotional benefit. Unlike some Maliki or Sufi-leaning interpretations that might prioritize the "state" of the listener, the Shafi'i school sticks to the literal implication of the hadith in Bukhari. Shafi'is also strictly distinguish between the *duff* (permitted by Sunnah) and other drums (like the *kuba* or long drum), which are prohibited via Qiyas because they were associated with effeminate men or specific sinful practices.

## Internal Dissent

**Opinion of Imam al-Ghazali (d. 1111):**
In his *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, Al-Ghazali argues that sounds (including music) are inherently neutral (*mubah*). He contends that the prohibition of instruments in the hadith was due to their association with "drinking parties" (*majalis al-shurb*) and the specific culture of the time, not because of the sounds themselves. He allows listening to music for those whose hearts are refined and directed toward Allah (the *Sama'* of the Sufis).
**Usul Reason for Divergence:** Ghazali employs a contextual reading of the *illah* (the reason) for prohibition, arguing it is "extrinsic" (*li-ghayrihi*) rather than "intrinsic" (*li-dhatihi*).
**Note:** Despite Ghazali's massive influence, the *mu'tamad* (official) position of the Shafi'i school—as solidified by Imam al-Nawawi and later Ibn Hajar al-Haytami in *Kaff al-Ra'a'*—remains the strict prohibition of the instruments themselves.

## Notes

- **Qawl Qadim vs. Qawl Jadid:** There is no significant change between Al-Shafi'i's Iraq and Egypt periods on this specific ruling; he remained consistent in his disapproval of music.
- **Geographic Variation:** In Southeast Asia (Shafi'i majority), there is a historical tension between the strict legal prohibition and local traditions (like Gamelan), often resolved by classifying certain instruments as "non-ma'azif" or through the "Ghazalian" exception for spiritual use, though the legal texts remain strict.
- **The Duff:** The permission for the *duff* is strictly for a drum without bells. If bells are added, it becomes a *mazhar* or *janj*, which the school generally prohibits.
