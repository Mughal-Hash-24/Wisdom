# Hanbali Position: What is the ruling on musical instruments?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - musical-instruments-ruling|Back to Synthesis]]

**School:** Hanbali
**Methodological Disposition:** Textualist. Strongest reliance on hadith corpus, including weak narrations; most resistant to independent qiyas and maslaha.
**Query Type:** [CLASSICAL POSITION]

---

## Ruling

[CLASSICAL POSITION] The dominant (mu'tamad) Hanbali position is that the use of musical instruments (ma'azif) is strictly prohibited (Haram), with the specific exception of the duff (a simple frame drum/tambourine) when used for weddings or similar permissible celebrations. Listening to music is likewise prohibited, and instruments that are used solely for entertainment are considered objects with no legal value; if broken, no compensation (daman) is required.

**Classical Reference:** *Al-Mughni* by Ibn Qudama (VERIFIED); *Al-Insaf* by Al-Mardawi (VERIFIED); *Kashshaf al-Qina'* by Al-Buhuti (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Sunnah and Sahaba Reports (Athar).

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Surah Luqman (Q 31:6) — "And of the people is he who buys the amusement of speech (lahw al-hadith) to mislead [others] from the way of Allah..."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** No specific single occasion of revelation recorded for the entire verse, but it is reported to refer to Nadr ibn al-Harith who bought singing girls to distract people from the Quran.
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* Open to interpretation (Mutashabih) regarding the specific meaning of "amusement of speech."
  - *Amm or Khass:* General (Amm) in its wording.
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation applies here.
- **Juristic Reading:** The Hanbali school relies heavily on the Tafsir of the Sahaba. Ibn Mas'ud and Ibn Abbas (Companions) both swore by Allah that "amusement of speech" refers to singing and music. For the Hanbali school, the interpretation of the Sahaba on the meaning of a Quranic term is given authoritative weight over purely linguistic or rationalist possibilities.

*If Sunnah:*
- **Hadith:** "There will be among my Ummah people who will seek to make lawful adultery, silk, alcohol, and musical instruments (ma'azif)..." — narrated by Abu Malik al-Ash'ari, reported in Sahih al-Bukhari (VERIFIED).
- **Authentication Status:** Sahih (Authentic). Although some later scholars questioned the connection (inqita') in Bukhari's chain, the Hanbali school accepts it as an authoritative proof-text (Hujjah).
- **Asbab al-Wurud:** A general prophecy/warning regarding the moral decay of the Ummah.
- **Scope:** General (Aam). The grouping of musical instruments with adultery and alcohol establishes a clear legal category of prohibition (Tahrim).
- **Application:** The term *ma'azif* is a generic plural covering all stringed, wind, and percussion instruments. The Hanbali school applies the literal breadth of the term to all instruments unless a specific exemption exists.

*If Sunnah (Exemption):*
- **Hadith:** The Prophet ﷺ allowed the beating of the *duff* at weddings and when he returned from battle. (VERIFIED)
- **Scope:** Specific (Khass) to the *duff* and specific to celebrations.
- **Application:** This acts as a *takhsis* (specification) of the general prohibition of instruments.

*If Qiyas (last resort):*
- **Why Qiyas Required:** Not required for the primary ruling, as textual evidence is deemed sufficient. However, Qiyas is used to extend the ruling of "no compensation" to modern iterations of instruments.
- **Original Case (Asl):** An idol or a container of wine.
- **New Case (Far'):** A lute (ud) or similar instrument.
- **Effective Cause (Illah):** Being a tool for a prohibited (Haram) purpose with no significant permissible use.
- **Extended Ruling (Hukm):** Lack of legal protection; no compensation for destruction.

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
The Hanbali school rejects the use of *Istihsan* (juristic preference) or *Maslaha Mursala* (public interest) to permit music for spiritual or psychological benefit (as some Sufi-leaning jurists might). The Hanbali position is that where a text (hadith) exists, human reasoning regarding the "utility" or "beauty" of the act is irrelevant. They diverge from the later Zahiri position (Ibn Hazm) by accepting the Bukhari hadith as authentic and the Sahaba's tafsir as binding.

---

## Internal Dissent

The most significant internal discussion involves the scope of the *duff*. While the standard position limits it to weddings and Eids for women/children, some Hanbali scholars (and later figures influenced by the school like Ibn Taymiyya) emphasized the distinction between "instruments of amusement" and "instruments of announcement." 

Ibn Taymiyya (VERIFIED) argued that the *duff* is permitted for "announcement" (i'lan) but maintained a strict stance against string and wind instruments, which he viewed as transforming the soul in a way that competes with the Quran. There is also a minority view within the school (mentioned in *Al-Insaf*) that suggests the *duff* might be permissible for all celebrations, not just weddings.

---

## Notes

The Hanbali ruling on music is notably strict because the school prioritizes the literal application of the "ma'azif" hadith. This ruling has been heavily cited and applied by 18th-20th century movements in the Najd region, leading to a public culture where instrumental music was historically suppressed in favor of vocal-only poetry or the simple *duff*.
