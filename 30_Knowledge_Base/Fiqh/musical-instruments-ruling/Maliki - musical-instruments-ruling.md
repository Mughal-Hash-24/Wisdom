# Maliki Position: What is the ruling on musical instruments?
[[T.O.C (Fiqh)|Up to Fiqh]] | [[Synthesis - musical-instruments-ruling|Back to Synthesis]]

**School:** Maliki
**Methodological Disposition:** Empiricist. Unique use of Medinan practice (amal ahl al-Madina) and maslaha mursala as independent legal sources.
**Query Type:** [CLASSICAL POSITION]

---

## Ruling

The dominant (mu'tamad) Maliki position is that the use of musical instruments—specifically stringed instruments (ma'azif) and wind instruments (mazamir)—is **prohibited (Haram)**. The use of the *Duff* (tambourine) is **permissible (Mubah)** or **recommended (Mandub)** specifically for weddings and Eid celebrations to announce the occasion, provided it is not accompanied by forbidden actions. Professional musicianship is considered a "lowly" trade that nullifies a person's legal uprightness ('adala) for the purpose of giving testimony in court.

**Classical Reference:** *Al-Mudawwana al-Kubra*, Sahnun (reporting from Ibn al-Qasim from Malik) (VERIFIED); *Al-Muwatta*, Malik ibn Anas (VERIFIED).

---

## Usul al-Fiqh — How This Ruling Was Derived

**Primary Legal Source:** Amal ahl al-Madina (Medinan Practice), Sunnah, and Maslaha Mursala.

**Derivation Chain:**

*If Quran:*
- **Verse:** Surah Luqman (Q 31:6) — "...purchases idle talk (lahw al-hadith) to mislead [others] from the way of Allah..."
- **Asbab al-Nuzul:** While some traditions link this to Nadr ibn al-Harith purchasing singing girls to distract people from the Quran, the Maliki school focuses on the effect of the act rather than a single historical incident.
- **Textual Analysis:**
  - *Muhkam or Mutashabih:* Mutashabih (Ambiguous) in literal wording, but clarified by the Sahaba.
  - *Amm or Khass:* Amm (General) regarding everything that distracts from God.
  - *Nasikh/Mansukh:* No abrogation applies here.
- **Juristic Reading:** The Maliki school follows the interpretation of Ibn Mas'ud and Ibn Abbas that "idle talk" specifically includes music that incites frivolity.

*If Sunnah:*
- **Hadith:** "There will be among my Ummah people who will seek to make lawful adultery, silk, alcohol, and musical instruments (ma'azif)." — narrated by Abu Amir or Abu Malik al-Ash'ari, reported in Sahih Bukhari (VERIFIED).
- **Authentication Status:** Sahih. While some later scholars (like Ibn Hazm) questioned the chain, the Maliki tradition accepts the prohibition implied by the grouping of instruments with adultery and alcohol.
- **Asbab al-Wurud:** General warning against the erosion of Sharia boundaries.
- **Scope:** General (Aam).
- **Application:** The grouping (iqtiran) of instruments with major sins indicates their prohibited nature.

*If Amal ahl al-Madina (Medinan Practice):*
- **The Practice:** Imam Malik observed that the people of knowledge and virtue in Medina avoided music. He was asked about the singing of the people of Medina and replied, "Only the transgressors (fussaq) among us do that."
- **Why This Source:** Malik treats the collective behavior of the people of Medina as a "living Sunnah" that outweighs isolated (ahad) hadiths if they conflict. The absence of instruments in the gatherings of the pious of Medina is a definitive proof of their disapproval.
- **Scope:** Universal school application based on Medinan precedent.

*If Maslaha Mursala (Public Interest):*
- **The Interest:** Protection of the spiritual heart and the social order.
- **Why Unattested:** Music is seen as a "precursor to fornication" (ruqyat al-zina) because it stirs the passions. The Maslaha lies in preventing the breakdown of modesty (muru’a).
- **Limits:** The prohibition is relaxed for the *Duff* and simple singing (huda') used by travelers or workers, as these serve a specific, non-frivolous need.

**Usul-Level Disputes:**
The Maliki school is more stringent than the Zahiri school (which permits music due to what they claim is a lack of authentic evidence) and more restrictive than certain Shafi'i views that might categorize it as Makruh (disliked) rather than Haram. The Maliki reliance on *'Amal* (practice) and *Sadd al-Dhara'i* (blocking the means) creates a higher threshold for prohibition than schools relying solely on textual literalism.

---

## Internal Dissent

There is a significant minority view among later Andalusian and Maghribi Malikis regarding "auditory pleasure" (sama'). Some, like **Ibn Bajjah** (though more a philosopher) and certain Sufi-leaning Maliki jurists, distinguished between instruments that incite lust and those that are purely melodic. However, the *Mu'tamad* (authoritative) position remains that of Malik in the *Mudawwana*: "If a man buys a slave-girl and finds she is a singer, he has the right to return her [to the seller] due to a defect." This confirms that music was legally categorized as a flaw or "defect" in a person's social and legal standing.

---

## Notes

In North and West Africa, where the Maliki school dominates, this ruling historically led to a distinction between "religious" percussion (like the drums used in certain Sufi orders, often debated) and "profane" stringed music. The Maliki emphasis on *Muru’a* (manliness/virtue) means that even if a specific instrument isn't explicitly named in a text, its impact on a person's dignity determines its legal status.
