# Proxenos

## Overview
**Proxenos** (Greek: πρόξενος, plural: *proxenoi*) was an ancient Greek institution — essentially the world's first diplomatic representatives, but with a twist.

## The Core Concept
A proxenos was a **citizen of City-State A** who officially represented the interests of **City-State B** within his own city. This is the inverse of modern ambassadors (who are citizens of the country they represent, stationed abroad).

**Example:** If Athens wanted representation in Corinth, they wouldn't send an Athenian to live there. Instead, they would appoint a *Corinthian citizen* — someone already living in Corinth, respected locally — to be their proxenos.

## Responsibilities
The proxenos served as:
1. **Diplomat** — Represented the foreign city's interests in political matters
2. **Host** — Provided hospitality to visiting citizens from the city he represented
3. **Legal Advocate** — Helped foreigners navigate local courts and bureaucracy
4. **Trade Facilitator** — Assisted merchants with commercial dealings
5. **Information Broker** — Relayed news and intelligence between cities

## Why This System?
Ancient Greece was a world of independent city-states (poleis) with no unified government. Each polis was sovereign. The proxenos system solved a practical problem:
- **Trust:** A local citizen had existing relationships and credibility
- **Access:** They could navigate local politics, attend assemblies, speak in courts
- **Permanence:** No need to maintain expensive foreign delegations

## The Honor
Being appointed proxenos was a **prestigious honor**. The foreign city would issue a formal decree (often inscribed in stone) granting the proxenos:
- **Proxenia** — The official title and role
- **Euergesia** — Recognition as a "benefactor"
- **Asylia** — Inviolability/protection
- **Ateleia** — Tax exemptions
- **Enktesis** — Right to own property in the foreign city
- **Proedria** — Front-row seats at festivals and games

## Historical Examples
- **Cimon of Athens** was proxenos for Sparta in Athens
- **Alcibiades' family** held proxenia for Sparta (though he later renounced it)
- Many wealthy citizens across Greece held multiple proxeniai

## Etymology
- **Pro-** (πρό) = "on behalf of" / "before"
- **Xenos** (ξένος) = "stranger" / "guest-friend"

So proxenos literally means "one who acts on behalf of a stranger/foreigner" — the public institutionalization of the Greek concept of *xenia* (guest-friendship).

## Legacy
The proxenos system is considered a precursor to:
- Modern consular services
- Honorary consuls (citizens of the host country representing a foreign nation)
- The concept of diplomatic immunity
